<p><b>Andrew Cardozo Fluegelman</b> (November 27, 1943 – <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> July 6, 1985) was a publisher, photographer, <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a> and attorney<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> best known as a pioneer of what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Shareware" title="Shareware">shareware</a> business model for software marketing. He was also the founding editor of both <i><a href="/wiki/PC_World_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="PC World (magazine)">PC World</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Macworld" title="Macworld">Macworld</a></i> and the leader of the 1970s <i><a href="/wiki/New_Games_Book" class="mw-redirect" title="New Games Book">New Games</a></i> movement, which advocated the development of <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">noncompetitive games</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_business_career">Early business career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early business career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publisher">Publisher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Publisher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1981 Fluegelman was the owner and sole employee of The Headlands Press, a small book publisher in <a href="/wiki/Tiburon,_California" title="Tiburon, California">Tiburon, California</a>. He had attended an early <a href="/wiki/Computer_expo" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer expo">computer expo</a> in San Francisco in the late 1970s, and after agreeing to publish and coauthor <i>Writing in the Computer Age</i> decided to purchase his first computer. In October Fluegelman received one of the first <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PCs</a> sold in San Francisco, and in two weeks began to write his own accounting program in <a href="/wiki/IBM_BASIC" title="IBM BASIC">IBM BASIC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hewes19820203_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hewes19820203-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Freeware.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Freeware&quot;">"Freeware"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Freeware&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late 1982 Fluegelman developed <a href="/wiki/PC-Talk" title="PC-Talk">PC-Talk</a>, a very popular and successful communications program.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He marketed it under a system he called "<a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">Freeware</a>", which he characterized as "an experiment in economics more than <a href="/wiki/Altruism" title="Altruism">altruism</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">Freeware</a> was licensed under terms that encouraged users to make voluntary payments for the software, and it allowed users to copy and redistribute the software freely as long as the license terms and text were not altered. He collaborated with PC-File (database software) developer Jim Knopf to adopt similar names (PC-File was originally "Easy-File"), and prices, for their initial shareware offerings; they also agreed to mention each other's products in their program's documentation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attorney">Attorney</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Attorney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluegelman was admitted to The State Bar of California in January 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magazine_editor">Magazine editor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Magazine editor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluegelman edited <a href="/wiki/PC_World_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="PC World (magazine)"><i>PC World</i></a> magazine from its introduction in 1982 until 1985, and <i><a href="/wiki/Macworld" title="Macworld">Macworld</a></i> magazine from its introduction in 1984 until 1985.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">Disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Fluegelman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1985, Fluegelman, already suffering from <a href="/wiki/Colitis" title="Colitis">colitis</a>, was diagnosed with cancer. On the afternoon of July 6, 1985, he left his office in <a href="/wiki/Tiburon,_California" title="Tiburon, California">Tiburon, California</a>. A week later, his abandoned car was found at the north end of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a> near San Francisco. His family held a memorial service for Fluegelman, and he is <a href="/wiki/Declared_death_in_absentia" class="mw-redirect" title="Declared death in absentia">presumed dead</a>, though his body has never been found. Kevin Strehlo, then an <i><a href="/wiki/InfoWorld" title="InfoWorld">InfoWorld</a></i> columnist, submitted a memorial column which mentioned that "friends say a <a href="/wiki/Suicide_note" title="Suicide note">suicide note</a> was found inside" his car. <i>InfoWorld</i> rejected this column, but an online news service published it.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
