	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >Mark Crispin</h1>
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		<div id="contentSub2"></div>
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Father of IMAP protocol</div>
<p><b>Mark Reed Crispin</b> (July 19, 1956 in <a href="/wiki/Camden,_New_Jersey" title="Camden, New Jersey">Camden, New Jersey</a> &#8211; December 28, 2012 in <a href="/wiki/Poulsbo,_Washington" title="Poulsbo, Washington">Poulsbo, Washington</a><sup id="cite_ref-CookObit_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CookObit-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is best known as the father of the <a href="/wiki/IMAP" class="mw-redirect" title="IMAP">IMAP</a> protocol, having invented it in 1985 during his time at the Stanford Knowledge Systems Laboratory. He is the author or co-author of numerous <a href="/wiki/Request_for_Comments" title="Request for Comments">RFCs</a>; and was the principal author of <a href="/wiki/UW_IMAP" title="UW IMAP">UW IMAP</a>, one of the reference implementations of the IMAP4rev1 protocol described in <b><a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3501">RFC 3501</a></b>. He also designed the <a href="/wiki/MIX_(email)" title="MIX (email)">MIX</a> mail storage format.
</p><p>Crispin earned a B.S. in Technology and Society from <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Institute_of_Technology" title="Stevens Institute of Technology">Stevens Institute of Technology</a> in 1977.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Crispin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1977 to 1988, he was a Systems Programmer at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>. He developed the first production <a href="/wiki/PDP-10" title="PDP-10">PDP-10</a> 32-bit address <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a> <a href="/wiki/Network_Control_Program" title="Network Control Program">Network Control Program</a> (NCP) for the <a href="/wiki/WAITS" title="WAITS">WAITS</a> operating system, and wrote or rewrote most of the WAITS ARPAnet protocol suite. Prior to that time most systems only supported the original 8-bit addresses. During that time, he wrote the infamous <b><a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc748">RFC 748</a></b>, the only document specifically marked in the RFC index with <i>note date of issue</i>; and a series of <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">Telnet</a> implementations for the <a href="/wiki/Incompatible_Timesharing_System" title="Incompatible Timesharing System">Incompatible Timesharing System</a>, <a href="/wiki/WAITS" title="WAITS">WAITS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/TOPS-20" title="TOPS-20">TOPS-20</a> operating systems whose escape behavior was playfully immortalized by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Steele" class="mw-redirect" title="Guy Steele">Guy Steele</a> in the April 1984 Communications of the ACM as <i>The Telnet Song</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1980s, shortly after becoming the Systems Programmer for the Stanford Computer Science Department's <a href="/wiki/TOPS-20" title="TOPS-20">TOPS-20</a> system, he became interested in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_mail" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic mail">electronic mail</a> software and systems; thereafter this became his primary focus. He became the principal developer of the <a href="/wiki/TOPS-20" title="TOPS-20">TOPS-20</a> mailsystem, and reportedly was still running TOPS-20 systems at his residence in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was at Stanford, in the 1985–88 period, that <a href="/wiki/IMAP" class="mw-redirect" title="IMAP">IMAP</a> was first developed.
</p><p>From 1988 to 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> he was a Software Engineer at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>, where much of the work in developing and popularizing <a href="/wiki/IMAP" class="mw-redirect" title="IMAP">IMAP</a> and building what became <a href="/wiki/UW_IMAP" title="UW IMAP">UW IMAP</a> was done. He forked UW IMAP into Panda IMAP<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> in May 2008.
</p><p>While working at UW, Mark was one of the creators of the simple and portable Unix email program Pine, launched in March 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, he wrote <b><a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc4042">RFC 4042</a></b>, his second <a href="/wiki/April_Fools%27_Day_RFC" class="mw-redirect" title="April Fools&#39; Day RFC">April Fools' Day RFC</a> describing <a href="/wiki/UTF-9_and_UTF-18" class="mw-redirect" title="UTF-9 and UTF-18">UTF-9 and UTF-18</a>, encodings of Unicode optimized for the PDP-10.
</p><p>In August 2008, Crispin joined <a href="/wiki/Messaging_Architects" title="Messaging Architects">Messaging Architects</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> as a Senior Software Engineer. At Messaging Architects, he wrote an entirely new IMAP server based upon a distributed mail store, and extended the <a href="/wiki/MIX_(email)" title="MIX (email)">MIX format</a> to support stubbing (via a mechanism called virtual mailboxes) and metadata.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Crispin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 19 November 2012, it was announced that Crispin was terminally ill and in hospice care,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and he died on December 28, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-CookObit_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CookObit-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2013, Crispin was posthumously awarded the Distinguished Alumni Award in Science and Technology by his alma mater, <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Institute_of_Technology" title="Stevens Institute of Technology">Stevens Institute of Technology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stevens2013_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stevens2013-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
