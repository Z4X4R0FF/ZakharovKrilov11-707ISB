<p><b>Robert Berriedale Keith Dewar</b> (21 June 1945 – 30 June 2015) was an English-born American <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and educator. He helped to develop <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a> and was an outspoken advocate of freely licensed <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a>. He was a cofounder, CEO, and president of <a href="/wiki/GNAT" title="GNAT">AdaCore</a> software company. He was also an enthusiastic amateur performer and musician, especially with the Village Light <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">Opera</a> Group in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Dewar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dewar was born in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, England, one of two sons of the theoretical chemist <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._S._Dewar" title="Michael J. S. Dewar">Michael J. S. Dewar</a> and Mary Dewar, née Williamson, a historian and scholar of English Tudor history.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1959, he moved with his parents from England to <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a>, when his father accepted a teaching job at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GSSobit_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSSobit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Dewar attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, earning a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (B.S.) in 1964, and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> (Ph.D.) in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He began to work with computers during graduate school.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Dewar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dewar was first Assistant Professor of Information Science and later Associate Professor of Computer Science at the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Institute_of_Technology" title="Illinois Institute of Technology">Illinois Institute of Technology</a> (IIT) from 1968 to 1975, before becoming Research Associate Professor of Computer Science at <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a> (NYU) in 1975, where he was Full Professor of Computer Science from 1976 to 2005, and becoming chair of the department.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IITobit_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IITobit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was involved with developing <a href="/wiki/International_standard" title="International standard">international standards</a> in programming and informatics, as a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Information_Processing" title="International Federation for Information Processing">International Federation for Information Processing</a> (IFIP) <a href="/wiki/IFIP_Working_Group_2.1" title="IFIP Working Group 2.1">IFIP Working Group 2.1</a> (WG 2.1) on Algorithmic Languages and Calculi,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specified</a>, maintains, and supports the languages <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_60" title="ALGOL 60">ALGOL 60</a> and <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He was involved in the design of ALGOL 68,<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and was WG 2.1 <a href="/wiki/Chairperson" title="Chairperson">chairperson</a> from 1978 to 1983.
</p><p>He was Associate Director of the <a href="/wiki/Courant_Institute_of_Mathematical_Sciences" title="Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences">Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences</a> from 1994 to 1997. Until his death, he was President of <a href="/wiki/GNAT" title="GNAT">AdaCore</a>, which he cofounded in 1994, and served as its CEO until 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Dewar was an outspoken advocate of freely licensed open-source software and an expert in copyright and patent law for software. He was in demand as a speaker at conferences and expert witness in legal actions.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_contributions">Software contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Dewar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Software contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While at the IIT, Dewar created the original <a href="/wiki/SPITBOL" title="SPITBOL">SPITBOL</a> compiler, with Ken Belcher in 1971, and Macro SPITBOL, with Tony McCann in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These implementations of <a href="/wiki/SNOBOL" title="SNOBOL">SNOBOL</a>4, which quickly gained widespread popularity, are still being used today.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the 1970s, he was a principal author of the Realia <a href="/wiki/COBOL" title="COBOL">COBOL</a> compiler,<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> today marketed by <a href="/wiki/Computer_Associates" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Associates">Computer Associates</a>, and still widely used in commercial environments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Dewar became involved with the language <a href="/wiki/Ada_(programming_language)" title="Ada (programming language)">Ada</a> from its early days as a Distinguished Reviewer of the Ada 1983 design proposed by <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ichbiah" title="Jean Ichbiah">Jean Ichbiah</a> that was selected by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> (US DoD).<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He was codirector, with Edmond Schonberg, of the team at NYU that produced Ada/Ed, an interpreter for Ada 83 written in <a href="/wiki/SETL" title="SETL">SETL</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the first Ada implementation to pass the strenuous ACVC validation suite,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> mandated for being allowed to use the trademarked name Ada.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dewar and Schonberg went on to produce <a href="/wiki/GNAT" title="GNAT">GNAT</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> compiler for Ada that forms part of the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Compiler_Collection" title="GNU Compiler Collection">GNU Compiler Collection</a> (GCC).<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Dewar also participated in the SETL project at NYU, and co-authored the handbook <i>Programming With Sets: An Introduction to SETL</i>. He influenced the design of the language <a href="/wiki/ABC_(programming_language)" title="ABC (programming language)">ABC</a>, in particular its SETL-style high-level <a href="/wiki/Data_type" title="Data type">data types</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Associative_array" title="Associative array">associative arrays</a>. <a href="/wiki/Guido_van_Rossum" title="Guido van Rossum">Guido van Rossum</a>, the author of the language <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a>, wrote that the use of the colon in Python is due to Dewar's wife.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Dewar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was married to Karin Dewar, née Anderson (died 2013), and had two children, Jenny (born 1965) and Keith (born 1969), and two grandchildren.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Dewar was known as an engaging and witty conversationalist.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dewar played the <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>, recorder, and other musical instruments and enjoyed singing. He was an enthusiastic and valued member and benefactor of the Village Light Opera Group (VLOG) for 35 years, serving them in many capacities, from producer and president to music director, and on stage from Harem Guard to the title role in <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-PRobit_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PRobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> VLOG's Dewar Center for the Performing Arts was named in recognition of Robert and Karin Dewar's contributions.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He was also a member of the North American <a href="/wiki/Heckelphone" title="Heckelphone">Heckelphone</a> Society<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and performed with other groups until only months before his death.<sup id="cite_ref-GSSobit_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSSobit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He died of cancer at age 70 at his home in <a href="/wiki/Bennington" class="mw-redirect" title="Bennington">Bennington</a>, Vermont.<sup id="cite_ref-GSSobit_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSSobit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYUobit_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYUobit-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
