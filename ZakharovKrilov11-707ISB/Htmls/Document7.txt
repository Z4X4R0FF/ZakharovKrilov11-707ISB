<p><b>Eric Paul Allman</b> (born September 2, 1955) is an American computer programmer who  developed <a href="/wiki/Sendmail" title="Sendmail">sendmail</a> and its precursor <a href="/wiki/Delivermail" title="Delivermail">delivermail</a> in the late 1970s and early 1980s at <a href="/wiki/UC_Berkeley" class="mw-redirect" title="UC Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a>. In 1998, Allman and Greg Olson co-founded the company <a href="/wiki/Sendmail,_Inc." title="Sendmail, Inc.">Sendmail, Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_training">Education and training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Allman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/El_Cerrito,_California" title="El Cerrito, California">El Cerrito, California</a>, Allman knew from an early age that he wanted to work in computing, breaking into his high school's <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe</a> and later using the UC Berkeley computing center for his computing needs. In 1973, he entered UC Berkeley, just as the <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> operating system began to become popular in academic circles.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  He earned B.S. and M.S. degrees from UC Berkeley in 1977 and 1980 respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sendmail_and_Syslog">Sendmail and Syslog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Allman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sendmail and Syslog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the Unix source code was available at Berkeley, the local <a href="/wiki/Hacker_culture" title="Hacker culture">hackers</a> quickly made many extensions to the <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a> code. One such extension was <i><a href="/wiki/Delivermail" title="Delivermail">delivermail</a></i>, which in 1981 turned into <i><a href="/wiki/Sendmail" title="Sendmail">sendmail</a></i>.  As an <a href="/wiki/Mail_transfer_agent" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail transfer agent">MTA</a>, it was designed to deliver <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> over the still relatively small (as compared to today's Internet) <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a>, which consisted of many smaller networks with vastly differing formats for e-mail headers.
</p><p>Sendmail soon became an important part of  the <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Software_Distribution" title="Berkeley Software Distribution">Berkeley Software Distribution</a> (BSD) and it used to be the most widely used <a href="/wiki/Message_transfer_agent" title="Message transfer agent">MTA</a> on Unix based systems, despite its somewhat complex configuration syntax and frequent abuse by Internet telemarketing firms. In 1998, Allman and Greg Olson founded <i>Sendmail, Inc.</i>, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Emeryville,_California" title="Emeryville, California">Emeryville, California</a>, to do <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a> work on improving <i>sendmail</i>.
</p><p>The logging format used by the MTA, known as <a href="/wiki/Syslog" title="Syslog">syslog</a>, was at first used solely by sendmail, but eventually became an unofficial standard format used by other unrelated programs for logging. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_contributions">Other contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Allman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allman is credited with popularizing the <a href="/wiki/Allman_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Allman style">Allman indent style</a>, also known as BSD indent style.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He ported a Fortran version of <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(1971_video_game)" title="Star Trek (1971 video game)">Super Star Trek</a> to the C programming language, which later became BSD Trek, and is still included in various Linux distributions as part of the classic bsdgames package.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was awarded the Telluride Tech Festival Award of Technology in August, 2006 in <a href="/wiki/Telluride,_Colorado" title="Telluride, Colorado">Telluride, Colorado</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and in 2009 he was recognized as a Distinguished Engineer by the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In April 2014 he was inducted into the Internet Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Allman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allman, who is openly gay, lives in <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Kirk_McKusick" title="Marshall Kirk McKusick">Marshall Kirk McKusick</a>, who had been his partner for more than 30 years before they married in October, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> McKusick is a lead developer of BSD; the two first met in graduate school.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
