<p><b>Neil Gunther</b> (born 15 August 1950) is a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> <a href="/wiki/Information_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Information systems">information systems</a> <a href="/wiki/Researcher" class="mw-redirect" title="Researcher">researcher</a> best known internationally for developing the <a href="/wiki/Open-source_license" title="Open-source license">open-source</a> <a href="/wiki/Performance_modeling" class="mw-redirect" title="Performance modeling">performance modeling</a> software <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pretty_Damn_Quick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pretty Damn Quick (page does not exist)">Pretty Damn Quick</a></i> and developing the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perfdynamics.com/iBook/gcap.html">Guerrilla approach</a> to computer capacity planning and performance analysis. He has also been cited for his contributions to the theory of large <a href="/wiki/Transient_(computer_programming)" title="Transient (computer programming)">transients</a> in <a href="/wiki/Computer_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer systems">computer systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Packet_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet network">packet networks</a>, and his <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.perfdynamics.com/Manifesto/USLscalability.html">universal law</a> of computational <a href="/wiki/Scalability" title="Scalability">scalability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gunther is a Senior Member of both the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> (ACM) and the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> (IEEE), as well as a member of the  <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a> (AMS), <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">American Physical Society</a> (APS), <a href="/wiki/Computer_Measurement_Group" title="Computer Measurement Group">Computer Measurement Group</a> (CMG) and ACM <a href="/wiki/SIGMETRICS" title="SIGMETRICS">SIGMETRICS</a>.
</p><p>He is currently focused on developing <a href="/wiki/Quantum_information" title="Quantum information">quantum information</a> system technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-njp_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-njp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gunther is an <a href="/wiki/Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian">Australian</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> and Scots ancestry, born in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> on 15 August 1950. He attended <a href="/w/index.php?title=Preston_East_Primary_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Preston East Primary School (page does not exist)">Preston East Primary School</a> from 1955 to 1956, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balwyn_North_Primary_School&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Balwyn North Primary School (page does not exist)">Balwyn North Primary School</a> from 1956 until 1962. For his tenth birthday, Gunther received a copy of the now famous book entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Book_of_Chemistry_Experiments" title="The Golden Book of Chemistry Experiments">The Golden Book of Chemistry Experiments</a></i> from an older cousin. Inspired by the book, he started working on various experiments, making use of various chemicals that could be found around in his house. After he spilled some <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a> solution on his bedroom carpet his mother confined him to an alcove in the garage which he turned into a small <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a>, replete with <a href="/wiki/Industrial_chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial chemical">industrial chemicals</a> and second-hand <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_glassware" title="Laboratory glassware">laboratory glassware</a>. Gunther was interested in finding out how things like <a href="/wiki/Detergents" class="mw-redirect" title="Detergents">detergents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Oils">oils</a> were composed by <i><a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a></i> them in his <a href="/wiki/Fractionating_column" title="Fractionating column">fractionating column</a>. He took particular interest in mixing paints for his art classes, as well as his chemistry classes in <a href="/wiki/Balwyn_High_School" title="Balwyn High School">Balwyn High School</a>. His father, being the Superintendent of Melbourne's <a href="/wiki/Electrical_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical power station">electrical power station</a>, borrowed an organic chemistry text from the chemists in the quality control laboratory. This ultimately led to an intense interest in synthesizing <a href="/wiki/Azo_compound" title="Azo compound">Azo dyes</a>. At around age 14, Gunther attempted to predict the color of azo dyes based on the <a href="/wiki/Chromophore" title="Chromophore">chromophore</a>-<a href="/wiki/Auxochrome" title="Auxochrome">auxochrome</a> combination. Apart from drawing up empirical tables, this effort was largely unsuccessful due to his lack of knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum theory</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Doc_years">Post-Doc years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Post-Doc years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gunther taught physics at <a href="/wiki/San_Jose_State_University" title="San Jose State University">San Jose State University</a> from 1980–1981.  He then joined <a href="/w/index.php?title=Syncal_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Syncal Corporation (page does not exist)">Syncal Corporation</a>, a small company contracted by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a href="/wiki/JPL" class="mw-redirect" title="JPL">JPL</a> to develop thermoelectric materials for their deep-space missions. Gunther was asked to analyze the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_stability" title="Thermal stability">thermal stability</a> test data from the <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">Voyager</a> <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">RTGs</a>. He discovered that the stability of the <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> (Si-Ge) <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermoelectric_alloy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thermoelectric alloy (page does not exist)">thermoelectric alloy</a> was controlled by a <a href="/wiki/Soliton" title="Soliton">soliton</a>-based precipitation mechanism.<sup id="cite_ref-SiGe_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SiGe-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> JPL used his work to  select the next generation of RTG materials for the <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft)" title="Galileo (spacecraft)">Galileo mission</a> launched in 1989.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Xerox_years">Xerox years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Xerox years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982, Gunther joined <a href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" class="mw-redirect" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> to develop parametric and functional test software for PARC's small-scale <a href="/wiki/VLSI" class="mw-redirect" title="VLSI">VLSI</a> design fabrication line. Ultimately, he was recruited onto the <i>Dragon</i> multiprocessor workstation project where he also developed the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=PARCbench&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="PARCbench (page does not exist)">PARCbench</a></i> multiprocessor benchmark. This was his first fore into computer performance analysis.
</p><p>1989, he developed a <a href="/wiki/Wick_rotation" title="Wick rotation">Wick-rotated</a> version of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Feynman" title="Richard Feynman">Richard Feynman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Path_integral_formulation" title="Path integral formulation">quantum path integral formalism</a> for analyzing performance degradation in large-scale computer systems and packet networks.<sup id="cite_ref-LrgTrans_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LrgTrans-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyramid_years">Pyramid years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pyramid years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990 Gunther joined <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Technology" title="Pyramid Technology">Pyramid Technology</a> (now part of Fujitsu Siemens Computers) where he held positions as Senior Scientist and Manager of the Performance Analysis Group that was responsible for attaining industry-high <a href="/wiki/Transaction_Processing_Performance_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Transaction Processing Performance Council">TPC</a> benchmarks on their <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> multiprocessors. He also performed simulations for the design of the <i>Reliant RM1000</i> parallel database server.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consulting_practice">Consulting practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consulting practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gunther founded <a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_Dynamics_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Performance Dynamics Company (page does not exist)">Performance Dynamics Company</a> as a sole proprietorship, registered in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 1994, to provide consulting and educational services for the management of high performance computer systems with an emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Profiling_(computer_programming)" title="Profiling (computer programming)">performance analysis</a> and enterprise-wide <a href="/wiki/Capacity_planning" title="Capacity planning">capacity planning</a>. He went on to release and develop his own <a href="/wiki/Open-source_license" title="Open-source license">open-source</a> <a href="/wiki/Profiling_(computer_programming)" title="Profiling (computer programming)">performance modeling</a> <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> called "PDQ (Pretty Damn Quick)" around 1998. That software also accompanied his first textbook on performance analysis entitled <i>The Practical Performance Analyst</i>. Several other books have followed since then.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_research_interests">Current research interests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Current research interests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_information_systems">Quantum information systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Quantum information systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Gunther has embarked on joint research into quantum information systems based on <a href="/wiki/Photonics" title="Photonics">photonics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-njp_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-njp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During the course of his research in this area, he has developed a theory of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Photon_bifurcation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Photon bifurcation (page does not exist)">photon bifurcation</a></i> that is currently being tested experimentally at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique_F%C3%A9d%C3%A9rale_de_Lausanne" title="École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne">École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spie_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spie-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This represents yet another application of <a href="/wiki/Path_integral_formulation" title="Path integral formulation">path integral formulation</a> to circumvent the <a href="/wiki/Wave-particle_duality" class="mw-redirect" title="Wave-particle duality">wave-particle duality</a> of light.
</p><p>In its simplest rendition, this theory can be considered as providing the <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> corrections to the <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Karl_Abbe" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernst Karl Abbe">Abbe</a>-<a href="/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Rayleigh</a> <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> theory of imaging and the <a href="/wiki/Fourier_optics" title="Fourier optics">Fourier theory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transforms" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourier transforms">optical information processing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOptics_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOptics-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_visualization">Performance visualization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Performance visualization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inspired by the work of <a href="/wiki/Tukey" class="mw-redirect" title="Tukey">Tukey</a>, Gunther explored ways to help systems analyst visualize performance in a manner similar to that already available in <a href="/wiki/Scientific_visualization" title="Scientific visualization">scientific visualization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_visualization" title="Information visualization">information visualization</a>. In 1991, he developed a tool called <i>Barry</i>, which employs <a href="/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Barycentric coordinates (mathematics)">barycentric coordinates</a> to visualize sampled <a href="/wiki/CPU_usage" class="mw-redirect" title="CPU usage">CPU usage</a> data on large-scale <a href="/wiki/Multiprocessor" class="mw-redirect" title="Multiprocessor">multiprocessor</a> systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Barry_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> More recently, he has applied the same 2-<a href="/wiki/Simplex" title="Simplex">simplex</a> barycentric coordinates to visualizing the <a href="/wiki/Apdex" title="Apdex">Apdex</a> application performance metric, which is based on <a href="/w/index.php?title=Categorical_response_time&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Categorical response time (page does not exist)">categorical response time</a> data. A barycentric 3-simplex (a <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron" title="Tetrahedron">tetrahedron</a>), that can be swivelled on the computer screen using a <a href="/wiki/Mouse_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mouse (computing)">mouse</a>, has been found useful for visualizing <a href="/wiki/Packet_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet network">packet network</a> performance data. In 2008, he co-founded the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://groups.google.com/group/perfviz?hl=en">PerfViz</a> <a href="/wiki/Google_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Google group">google group</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Universal_Law_of_Computational_Scalability">Universal Law of Computational Scalability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neil_J._Gunther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Universal Law of Computational Scalability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

This scalability law was originally developed by Gunther in 1993 while he was employed at <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Technology" title="Pyramid Technology">Pyramid Technology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UniScaling_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UniScaling-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Since there are no <a href="/wiki/Topological" class="mw-redirect" title="Topological">topological</a> dependencies, C(N) can model <a href="/wiki/Symmetric_multiprocessing" title="Symmetric multiprocessing">symmetric multiprocessors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multi-core_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-core (computing)">multicores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cluster_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cluster (computing)">clusters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grid_computing" title="Grid computing">GRID</a> architectures. Also, because each of the three terms has a definite physical meaning, they can be employed as a <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">heuristic</a> to determine where to make performance improvements in hardware platforms or software applications.
</p><p>At a more fundamental level, the above equation can be derived<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> from the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Machine_Repairman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Machine Repairman (page does not exist)">Machine Repairman</a> queueing model:<sup id="cite_ref-Gross_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gross-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Theorem (Gunther 2008):</b> The universal scalability law is equivalent to the synchronous queueing bound on throughput in a modified Machine Repairman with state-dependent service times.
</p>
</blockquote>
