<p><b>David Neil Cutler Sr.</b> (born March 13, 1942) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>.  He developed several computer <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, namely <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows_NT" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsoft Windows NT">Microsoft Windows NT</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/RSX-11M" class="mw-redirect" title="RSX-11M">RSX-11M</a>, <a href="/wiki/VAXELN" title="VAXELN">VAXELN</a> and <a href="/wiki/OpenVMS" title="OpenVMS">VMS (now OpenVMS)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-techrecog_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techrecog-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_history">Personal history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cutler was born in <a href="/wiki/Lansing,_Michigan" title="Lansing, Michigan">Lansing, Michigan</a> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/DeWitt,_Michigan" title="DeWitt, Michigan">DeWitt, Michigan</a>. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Olivet_College" title="Olivet College">Olivet College</a>, Michigan, in 1965, he went to work for <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>.
</p><p>Cutler holds at least 20 patents, and is affiliate faculty in the Computer Science Department at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cutler is an avid <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">auto racing</a> driver.  He competed in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Championship" title="Atlantic Championship">Atlantic Championship</a> from 1996 to 2002, scoring a career best of 8th on the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Mile" title="Milwaukee Mile">Milwaukee Mile</a> in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-techrecog_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-techrecog-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cutler is a member of Adelphic Alpha Pi Fraternity at <a href="/wiki/Olivet_College" title="Olivet College">Olivet College</a>, Michigan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="DuPont_.281965_to_1971.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="DuPont_(1965_to_1971)">DuPont (1965 to 1971)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: DuPont (1965 to 1971)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cutler's first exposure to computers came when he was tasked to perform a <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulations</a> model for one of DuPont's customers using IBM's <a href="/wiki/GPSS" title="GPSS"> GPSS-3</a> language on an <a href="/wiki/IBM_7040" title="IBM 7040">IBM model 7044</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This work led to an interest in how computers and their operating systems worked.
</p>
<h2><span id="Digital_Equipment_Corporation_.281971_to_1988.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_Equipment_Corporation_(1971_to_1988)">Digital Equipment Corporation (1971 to 1988)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Digital Equipment Corporation (1971 to 1988)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cutler left DuPont to pursue his interest in computer systems, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> in 1971. He worked at the famous "Mill" facility in <a href="/wiki/Maynard,_Massachusetts" title="Maynard, Massachusetts">Maynard, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RSX-11M">RSX-11M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: RSX-11M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VMS">VMS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: VMS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 1975, Digital began a hardware project, code-named <i>Star,</i> to design a 32-bit virtual address extension to its <a href="/wiki/PDP-11" title="PDP-11">PDP-11</a>. In June 1975, Cutler, together with <a href="/wiki/Dick_Hustvedt" title="Dick Hustvedt">Dick Hustvedt</a> and Peter Lipman, were appointed the technical project leaders for the software project, code-named <i>Starlet,</i> to develop a totally new operating system for the Star family of processors. These two projects were tightly integrated from the beginning.
</p><p>The three technical leaders of the Starlet project together with three technical leaders of the Star project formed the "Blue Ribbon Committee" at Digital who produced the fifth design evolution for the programs. The design featured simplifications to the memory management and process scheduling schemes of the earlier proposals and the architecture was accepted. The Star and Starlet projects culminated in the development of the <a href="/wiki/VAX-11/780" class="mw-redirect" title="VAX-11/780">VAX-11/780</a> superminicomputer and the <a href="/wiki/VAX/VMS" class="mw-redirect" title="VAX/VMS">VAX/VMS</a> operating system, respectively.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prism_and_Mica_projects">Prism and Mica projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Prism and Mica projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Digital began working on a new CPU using <a href="/wiki/RISC" class="mw-redirect" title="RISC">RISC</a> design principles in 1986. Cutler, who was working in DEC's DECwest facility in Bellevue, Washington, was selected to head <a href="/wiki/DEC_Prism" title="DEC Prism">Prism</a>, a project to develop the company's RISC machine. Its operating system, code named <a href="/wiki/DEC_Prism#Mica" title="DEC Prism">Mica</a>, was to embody the next generation of design principles and have a compatibility layer for Unix and VMS.  The RISC machine was to be based on <a href="/wiki/Emitter_coupled_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Emitter coupled logic">ECL</a> technology, and was one of three ECL projects Digital was undertaking at the time.
</p><p>Funding the Research and Development (R&amp;D) costs of multiple ECL projects yielding products that would ultimately compete against each other was a strain. Of the three ECL projects, the VAX 9000 was the only one that was directly commercialized.  Primarily because of the early successes of the <a href="/wiki/DECstation" title="DECstation">PMAX</a> advanced development project and the need for differing business models, Prism was canceled in 1988 in favor of PMAX.
</p><p>Prism later surfaced as the basis of Digital's <a href="/wiki/DEC_Alpha" title="DEC Alpha">Alpha</a> family of computer systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attitude_towards_Unix">Attitude towards Unix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Attitude towards Unix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cutler is known for his disdain for all things <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>.  His sardonic nature showed through in the VMS versus Unix debates at Digital in the early 1980s.  He expressed his low opinion of the Unix process input/output model by reciting "Get a byte, get a byte, get a byte byte byte" to the tune of the finale of Rossini's <a href="/wiki/William_Tell_Overture" title="William Tell Overture"><i>William Tell</i> Overture</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Microsoft_.281988_to_Present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Microsoft_(1988_to_Present)">Microsoft (1988 to Present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Microsoft (1988 to Present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microsoft_Windows_NT">Microsoft Windows NT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Microsoft Windows NT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cutler left Digital for Microsoft in October 1988 and led the development of Windows NT. Later, he worked on targeting Windows NT to Digital's 64-bit <a href="/wiki/DEC_Alpha" title="DEC Alpha">Alpha</a> architecture then on <a href="/wiki/Windows_2000" title="Windows 2000">Windows 2000</a>. After the demise of Windows on Alpha (and the demise of Digital), he was instrumental in porting Windows to AMD's new 64-bit <a href="/wiki/AMD64" class="mw-redirect" title="AMD64">AMD64</a> architecture. He was officially involved with the <a href="/wiki/Windows_XP" title="Windows XP">Windows XP</a> Pro 64-bit and <a href="/wiki/Windows_Server" title="Windows Server">Windows Server</a> 2003 SP1 64-bit releases. He moved to working on Microsoft's Live Platform in August 2006. Dave Cutler was awarded the prestigious status of Senior <a href="/wiki/Fellow#Industry_and_corporate" title="Fellow">Technical Fellow</a> at Microsoft.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microsoft_Windows_Azure">Microsoft Windows Azure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Microsoft Windows Azure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Professional_Developers_Conference" title="Professional Developers Conference">Professional Developers Conference</a>, Microsoft announced <a href="/wiki/Azure_Services_Platform" class="mw-redirect" title="Azure Services Platform">Azure Services Platform</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud-based</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> which Microsoft is developing. During the conference keynote, Cutler was mentioned as a lead developer on the project, along with Amitabh Srivastava.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microsoft_Xbox">Microsoft Xbox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Microsoft Xbox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of January&#160;2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, a spokesperson for Microsoft confirmed that Cutler was no longer working on Windows Azure, and had joined the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> team.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> No further information was provided as to what Cutler's role was, nor what he was working on within the team.
</p><p>In May 2013, Microsoft announced the Xbox One console, and Cutler was mentioned as having worked in the development of host OS portion of the system running inside the new gaming device. Apparently his work was focused in creating an optimized version of Microsoft's Hyper-V Host OS specifically designed for Xbox One.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Cutler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Recognized as a 2007 National Medal of Technology and Innovation Laureate, awarded on 29 September 2008 at a White House ceremony in Washington, DC.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Honored as a Computer History Museum Fellow on 16 April 2016 at the Computer History Museum in Mountain View, California.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
