<p><b>Alfred Vaino Aho</b> (born August 9, 1941) is a Canadian <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> best known for his work on <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a>, and related algorithms, and his textbooks on the art and science of computer programming.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acm_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acm-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Aho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aho received a B.A.Sc. in Engineering Physics from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> and a Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering/Computer Science from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>. He conducted research at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> from 1967 to 1991, and again from 1997 to 2002 as Vice President of the Computing Sciences Research Center. As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Aho&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> he holds the Lawrence Gussman Chair of <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Science">Computer Science</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>. He served as chair of the department from 1995 to 1997, and again in the spring of 2003.
</p><p>In his PhD thesis Aho created <a href="/wiki/Indexed_grammar" title="Indexed grammar">indexed grammars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nested_stack_automaton" title="Nested stack automaton">nested-stack automaton</a> as vehicles for extending the power of <a href="/wiki/Context-free_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Context-free languages">context-free languages</a>, but retaining many of their decidability and closure properties. Indexed grammars have been used<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2011)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to model parallel rewriting systems, particularly in biological applications.
</p><p>After graduating from Princeton, Aho joined the Computing Sciences Research Center at Bell Labs where he devised efficient regular expression and string-pattern matching algorithms that he implemented in the first versions of the <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> tools <code><a href="/wiki/Egrep" class="mw-redirect" title="Egrep">egrep</a></code> and <code><a href="/wiki/Fgrep" class="mw-redirect" title="Fgrep">fgrep</a></code>.  The <code>fgrep</code> algorithm has become known as the <a href="/wiki/Aho-Corasick_algorithm" class="mw-redirect" title="Aho-Corasick algorithm">Aho-Corasick algorithm</a>; it is used by several bibliographic search-systems, including the one developed by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_J._Corasick" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret J. Corasick">Margaret J. Corasick</a>, and by other string-searching applications.
</p><p>At Bell Labs, Aho worked closely with <a href="/wiki/Stephen_C._Johnson" title="Stephen C. Johnson">Steve Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Ullman" title="Jeffrey Ullman">Jeffrey Ullman</a> to develop efficient algorithms for analyzing and translating programming languages. Steve Johnson used the bottom-up LALR parsing algorithms to create the syntax-analyzer generator <a href="/wiki/Yacc" title="Yacc">yacc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Lesk" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael E. Lesk">Michael E. Lesk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Schmidt" title="Eric Schmidt">Eric Schmidt</a> used Aho's regular-expression pattern-matching algorithms to create the lexical-analyzer generator <a href="/wiki/Lex_(software)" title="Lex (software)">lex</a>. The lex and yacc tools and their derivatives have been used to develop the front ends of many of today's programming language compilers.
</p><p>Aho and Ullman wrote a series of textbooks on compiling techniques that codified the theory relevant to compiler design. Their 1977 textbook <i><a href="/wiki/Principles_of_Compiler_Design" title="Principles of Compiler Design">Principles of Compiler Design</a></i> had a green dragon on the front cover and became known as "the green dragon book". In 1986 Aho and Ullman were joined by <a href="/wiki/Ravi_Sethi" title="Ravi Sethi">Ravi Sethi</a> to create a new edition, "the red dragon book" (which was briefly shown in the 1995 movie "<a href="/wiki/Hackers_(film)" title="Hackers (film)">Hackers</a>"), and in 2007 also by Monica Lam to create "the purple dragon book". The dragon books have been the most widely used compiler textbooks throughout the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1974, Aho, <a href="/wiki/John_Hopcroft" title="John Hopcroft">John Hopcroft</a>, and Ullman wrote the <i>Design and Analysis of Computer Algorithms</i>, codifying some of their early research on algorithms. This book became one of the most highly cited books in computer science for several decades and helped to stimulate the creation of algorithms and data structures as a central course in the computer science curriculum.
</p><p>Aho is also widely known for his co-authorship of the <a href="/wiki/AWK_programming_language" class="mw-redirect" title="AWK programming language">AWK programming language</a> with <a href="/wiki/Peter_J._Weinberger" title="Peter J. Weinberger">Peter J. Weinberger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Kernighan" title="Brian Kernighan">Brian Kernighan</a> (the "A" stands for "Aho"). As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Aho&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> Aho's research interests include programming languages, compilers, algorithms, and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computing" title="Quantum computing">quantum computing</a>. He is part of the Language and Compilers research-group at Columbia University.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Overall, his works have been cited 81,040 times and he has an <a href="/wiki/H-index" title="H-index">h-index</a> of 66, as of May 8, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aho has received many prestigious honors, including the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">IEEE</a>'s <a href="/wiki/IEEE_John_von_Neumann_Medal" title="IEEE John von Neumann Medal">John von Neumann Medal</a> and membership in the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a>. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He holds honorary doctorates from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Waterloo" title="University of Waterloo">University of Waterloo</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>, and is a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">ACM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IEEE" class="mw-redirect" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>.  
</p><p>Aho has twice served as chair of the Advisory Committee for the Computer and Information Science and Engineering Directorate of the National Science Foundation. He is a past president of the <a href="/wiki/ACM_Special_Interest_Group_on_Algorithms_and_Computability_Theory" class="mw-redirect" title="ACM Special Interest Group on Algorithms and Computability Theory">ACM Special Interest Group on Algorithms and Computability Theory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching">Teaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alfred_Aho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Teaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aho has taught at Columbia University in the City of New York since 1995. He won the Great Teacher Award from the Society of Columbia Graduates in 2003.
</p>
