	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >William Crowther (programmer)</h1>

<p><b>William</b> (<b>"Willie"</b> or <b>"Will"</b>) <b>Crowther</b> (born 1936) is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caving" title="Caving">caver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rock_Climbing" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock Climbing">rock climber</a>. He is best known as the co-creator of <i><a href="/wiki/Colossal_Cave_Adventure" title="Colossal Cave Adventure">Colossal Cave Adventure</a></i>, a seminal computer game that influenced the first decade of <a href="/wiki/Computer_game_designer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game designer">game design</a> and inspired the <a href="/wiki/Text_adventure" class="mw-redirect" title="Text adventure">text adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Game_genres" class="mw-redirect" title="Game genres">game genre</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Crowther_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IMP_Team.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/IMP_Team.jpg/300px-IMP_Team.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/IMP_Team.jpg/450px-IMP_Team.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/IMP_Team.jpg/600px-IMP_Team.jpg 2x" data-file-width="659" data-file-height="340" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:IMP_Team.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>IMP Team (left to right): Truett Thatch, Bill Bartell, Dave Walden, Jim Geisman, Robert Kahn, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Heart" title="Frank Heart">Frank Heart</a>, Ben Barker, Marty Thorpe, Will Crowther, Severo Ornstein</div></div></div>
<p>During the early 1970s, Crowther worked at <a href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense contractor">defense contractor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> pioneer <a href="/wiki/Bolt_Beranek_and_Newman" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolt Beranek and Newman">Bolt Beranek and Newman</a> (B.B.N.), where he was part of the original small <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPAnet</a> development team.  His implementation of a distributed distance vector routing system for the ARPAnet was an important step in the evolution of the internet.
</p><p>Crowther met and married <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Crowther_(caver)" title="Patricia Crowther (caver)">Pat Crowther</a> while studying at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, where he received a <a href="/wiki/B.S." class="mw-redirect" title="B.S.">B.S.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-interviewJudy_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-interviewJudy-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adventure">Adventure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Crowther_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Adventure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following his divorce from his wife, Crowther used his spare time to develop a text-based adventure game in <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> on BBN's <a href="/wiki/PDP-10" title="PDP-10">PDP-10</a>.  He created it as a diversion his daughters Sandy and Laura could enjoy when they came to visit.<sup id="cite_ref-Montfort2005-8597_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montfort2005-8597-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crowther wrote:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I had been involved in a non-computer role-playing game called Dungeons and
Dragons at the time, and also I had been actively exploring in caves - Mammoth
Cave in Kentucky in particular. Suddenly, I got involved in a divorce, and that
left me a bit pulled apart in various ways. In particular I was missing my
kids. Also the caving had stopped, because that had become awkward, so I
decided I would fool around and write a program that was a re-creation in fantasy of my caving, and also would be a game for the kids, and perhaps some
aspects of the Dungeons and Dragons that I had been playing.
My idea was that it would be a computer game that would not be intimidating to
non-computer people, and that was one of the reasons why I made it so that the
player directs the game with natural language input, instead of more
standardized commands. My kids thought it was a lot of fun.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson1983_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson1983-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Colossal_Cave_Adventure" title="Colossal Cave Adventure">Colossal Cave</a>, or more simply called <a href="/wiki/Colossal_Cave_Adventure" title="Colossal Cave Adventure"><i>Adventure</i></a>, the player moves around an imaginary cave system by entering simple, two-word commands and reading text describing the result.  Crowther used his extensive knowledge of cave exploration as a basis for the game play, and there are many similarities between the locations in the game and those in Mammoth Cave, particularly its <a href="/wiki/Bedquilt" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedquilt">Bedquilt</a> section.<sup id="cite_ref-Montfort2005-88_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montfort2005-88-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  In 1975 Crowther released the game on the early <a href="/wiki/ARPAnet" class="mw-redirect" title="ARPAnet">ARPAnet</a> system, of which BBN was a prime contractor.<sup id="cite_ref-Montfort2005-89_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montfort2005-89-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the spring of 1976, he was contacted by Stanford researcher <a href="/wiki/Don_Woods_(programmer)" title="Don Woods (programmer)">Don Woods</a>, seeking his permission to enhance the game.  Crowther agreed, and Woods developed several enhanced versions on a PDP-10 housed in the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory</a> (SAIL) where he worked.<sup id="cite_ref-Montfort2005-89_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montfort2005-89-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Over the following decade the game gained in popularity, being ported to many <a href="/wiki/Operating_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating systems">operating systems</a>, including personal-computer platform <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a>.
</p><p>The basic game structure invented by Crowther (and based in part on the example of the <a href="/wiki/ELIZA" title="ELIZA">ELIZA</a> text <a href="/wiki/Parser" class="mw-redirect" title="Parser">parser</a>) was carried forward by the designers of later adventure games.  <a href="/wiki/Marc_Blank" title="Marc Blank">Marc Blank</a> and the team that created the <a href="/wiki/Zork" title="Zork">Zork</a> adventures cite <i>Adventure</i> as the title that inspired them to create their game.  They later founded <a href="/wiki/Infocom" title="Infocom">Infocom</a> and published a series of popular text adventures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caving">Caving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Crowther_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Caving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The location of the game in <i>Colossal Cave</i> was not a coincidence.  Crowther and his first wife Pat were active and dedicated cavers in the 1960s and early 1970s&#8212;both were part of many expeditions to connect the <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_Cave" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammoth Cave">Mammoth</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flint_Ridge_Cave&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Flint Ridge Cave (page does not exist)">Flint Ridge</a> cave systems.  Pat played a key role in the September 9, 1972 expedition that finally <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_Cave_National_Park#Flint–Mammoth_connection_(1972)" title="Mammoth Cave National Park">made the connection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BruckerWatson1987_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BruckerWatson1987-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Indeed, even during his time working at BBN, his colleagues noticed that Crowther spent a fair amount of time doing chin-ups in doorframes, which apparently helped him concentrate.<sup id="cite_ref-HafnerLyon1998_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HafnerLyon1998-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=MIT_Outing_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MIT Outing Club (page does not exist)">MIT Outing Club</a> during the late 1950s and early 1960s, Crowther also played an important role in the development of rock climbing in the <a href="/wiki/Shawangunks" class="mw-redirect" title="Shawangunks">Shawangunks</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_State" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State">New York State</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Waterman2018_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waterman2018-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  He began climbing there in the 1950s and continues to climb today.  He made the first ascent of several classic routes including Arrow, Hawk, Moonlight, and Senté.  Some of these routes sparked controversy because protection bolts were placed on rappel, a new tactic that Crowther and several others began to use at the time.  The community reaction to this technique was an important part of the evolution of climbing ethics in the Shawangunks and beyond.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rest_of_career">Rest of career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Crowther_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rest of career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crowther worked at <a href="/wiki/Xerox_PARC" class="mw-redirect" title="Xerox PARC">Xerox PARC</a> from 1976 to 1983.  During this period he met and married Nancy Sanders Burnes in 1980 in Palo Alto, California.  The two of them did a lot of rock climbing with friends in Yosemite and elsewhere.  In 1983 he left Xerox and went back to Bolt Beranek and Newman in Cambridge, Massachusetts.  He became active with the Appalachian Mountain Club and every year helped teach rock climbing to beginners.  He continues doing that each spring, with the most recent session being held in spring of 2013.
</p><p>In the 1990s <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a> bought the part of BBN where Crowther was working.  He continued to work for Cisco and retired from there in 1997.  He now lives in Delanson, New York, with his second wife Nancy.
</p>
