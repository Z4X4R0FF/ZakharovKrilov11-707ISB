<p><b>John Gilmore</b> (born 1955) is one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cypherpunk" title="Cypherpunk">Cypherpunks</a> mailing list, and <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_Solutions" title="Cygnus Solutions">Cygnus Solutions</a>. He created the <a href="/wiki/Alt.*_hierarchy" title="Alt.* hierarchy"><i>alt.*</i> hierarchy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Usenet" title="Usenet">Usenet</a> and is a major contributor to the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Project" title="GNU Project">GNU Project</a>.
</p><p>An outspoken <a href="/wiki/Civil_libertarianism" title="Civil libertarianism">civil libertarian</a>, Gilmore has <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">sued</a> the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice</a>, and others. He was the plaintiff in the prominent case <i><a href="/wiki/Gilmore_v._Gonzales" title="Gilmore v. Gonzales">Gilmore v. Gonzales</a></i>, challenging <a href="/wiki/Secret_law" title="Secret law">secret travel-restriction laws</a>. He is also an advocate for <a href="/wiki/Drug_policy_reform" title="Drug policy reform">drug policy reform</a>.
</p><p>He co-authored the <a href="/wiki/Bootstrap_Protocol" title="Bootstrap Protocol">Bootstrap Protocol</a> in 1985, which evolved into <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol" title="Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol">Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol</a> (DHCP), the primary way local networks assign an <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> to devices.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Gilmore_(activist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the fifth employee of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> and founder of <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_Solutions" title="Cygnus Solutions">Cygnus Support</a>, he became wealthy enough to retire early and pursue other interests.
</p><p>He is a frequent contributor to <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a>, and worked on several <a href="/wiki/GNU" title="GNU">GNU</a> projects, including maintaining the <a href="/wiki/GNU_Debugger" title="GNU Debugger">GNU Debugger</a> in the early 1990s, initiating <a href="/wiki/GNU_Radio" title="GNU Radio">GNU Radio</a> in 1998, starting <a href="/wiki/Gnash_(software)" title="Gnash (software)">Gnash</a> media player in December 2005 to create a free software player for <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a> movies, and writing the pdtar program which became GNU <a href="/wiki/Tar_(file_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tar (file format)">tar</a>.  Outside of the GNU project he founded the <a href="/wiki/FreeS/WAN" title="FreeS/WAN">FreeS/WAN</a> project, an implementation of <a href="/wiki/IPsec" title="IPsec">IPsec</a>, to promote the encryption of Internet traffic.  He sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">EFF</a>'s <a href="/wiki/EFF_DES_cracker" title="EFF DES cracker">Deep Crack</a> <a href="/wiki/Data_Encryption_Standard" title="Data Encryption Standard">DES</a> cracker, sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Micropolis_(software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Micropolis (software)">Micropolis</a> city building game based on <a href="/wiki/SimCity_(1989_video_game)" title="SimCity (1989 video game)">SimCity</a>, and is a proponent of <a href="/wiki/Opportunistic_encryption" title="Opportunistic encryption">opportunistic encryption</a>.
</p><p>Gilmore co-authored the <a href="/wiki/Bootstrap_Protocol" title="Bootstrap Protocol">Bootstrap Protocol</a> (<a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc951">RFC 951</a>) with Bill Croft in 1985. The Bootstrap Protocol evolved into <a href="/wiki/DHCP" class="mw-redirect" title="DHCP">DHCP</a>, the method by which Ethernet and wireless networks typically assign devices an <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activism">Activism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Gilmore_(activist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Activism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gilmore owns the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> toad.com, which is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_oldest_currently_registered_Internet_domain_names" title="List of the oldest currently registered Internet domain names">100 oldest</a> active <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a> domains. It was registered on August 18, 1987. He runs<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the <a href="/wiki/Message_transfer_agent" title="Message transfer agent">mail server</a> at toad.com as an <a href="/wiki/Open_mail_relay" title="Open mail relay">open mail relay</a>. In October 2002, Gilmore's ISP, <a href="/wiki/Verio" title="Verio">Verio</a>, cut off his Internet access for running an open relay, a violation of Verio's terms of service. Many people contend that open relays make it too easy to send spam. Gilmore protests that his mail server was programmed to be essentially useless to <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">spammers</a> and other senders of mass email and he argues that Verio's actions constitute censorship. He also notes that his configuration makes it easier for friends who travel to send email, although his critics counter that there are other mechanisms to accommodate people wanting to send email while traveling. The measures Gilmore took to make his server useless to spammers may or may not have helped, considering that in 2002, at least one mass-mailing worm that propagated through open relays&#160;— W32.Yaha&#160;— had been hard-coded to relay through the toad.com mail server.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gilmore famously stated of <a href="/wiki/Internet_censorship" title="Internet censorship">Internet censorship</a> that "The Net interprets censorship as damage and routes around it".<sup id="cite_ref-Censorship_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Censorship-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He unsuccessfully challenged the constitutionality of <a href="/wiki/Secret_law" title="Secret law">secret regulations</a> regarding travel security policies in <i><a href="/wiki/Gilmore_v._Gonzales" title="Gilmore v. Gonzales">Gilmore v. Gonzales</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-UST_2004_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UST_2004-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SFC_2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFC_2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gilmore is also an advocate for the relaxing of <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_of_drugs" title="Prohibition of drugs">drug laws</a> and has given financial support to <a href="/wiki/Students_for_Sensible_Drug_Policy" title="Students for Sensible Drug Policy">Students for Sensible Drug Policy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marijuana_Policy_Project" title="Marijuana Policy Project">Marijuana Policy Project</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erowid" title="Erowid">Erowid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_Association_for_Psychedelic_Studies" title="Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies">MAPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flex_Your_Rights" title="Flex Your Rights">Flex Your Rights</a>, and various other organizations seeking to end the <a href="/wiki/War_on_drugs" title="War on drugs">war on drugs</a>. He is a member of the boards of directors of MAPS, the Marijuana Policy Project, and the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliations">Affiliations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Gilmore_(activist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Affiliations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the sale of <a href="/wiki/AMPRNet" title="AMPRNet">AMPRNet</a> address space in mid-2019, Gilmore, under <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_call_signs" title="Amateur radio call signs">amateur radio call sign</a> W0GNU, was listed as a board member of <a href="/wiki/AMPRNet#Amateur_Radio_Digital_Communications_(non-profit)" title="AMPRNet">Amateur Radio Digital Communications, Inc.</a>, a non-profit involved in the management of the <a href="/wiki/AMPRNet" title="AMPRNet"><i><span class="ipaddr"><span style="padding-right: 1px;">44.0.0.0</span><span style="padding-right: 1px;">/</span><span style="padding-right: 1px;">8</span></span></i></a> resources on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> community.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amprnet-20190720_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amprnet-20190720-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Gilmore_(activist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>
