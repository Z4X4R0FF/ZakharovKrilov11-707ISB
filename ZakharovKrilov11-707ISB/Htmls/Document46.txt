<p><b>Edgar Frank</b> "<b>Ted</b>" <b>Codd</b> (19 August 1923 – 18 April 2003) was an English <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer scientist</a> who, while working for <a href="/wiki/International_Business_Machines" class="mw-redirect" title="International Business Machines">IBM</a>, invented the <a href="/wiki/Relational_model" title="Relational model">relational model</a> for <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a> management, the theoretical basis for <a href="/wiki/Relational_database" title="Relational database">relational databases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relational_database_management_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Relational database management system">relational database management systems</a>. He made other valuable contributions to <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, but the relational model, a very influential general theory of data management, remains his most mentioned, analyzed and celebrated achievement.<sup id="cite_ref-dblp_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acm_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edgar_F._Codd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edgar Frank Codd was born in <a href="/wiki/Fortuneswell" title="Fortuneswell">Fortuneswell</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Isle of Portland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, England. After attending <a href="/wiki/Poole_Grammar_School" title="Poole Grammar School">Poole Grammar School</a>, he studied mathematics and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> at <a href="/wiki/Exeter_College,_Oxford" title="Exeter College, Oxford">Exeter College, Oxford</a>, before serving as a pilot in the <a href="/wiki/RAF_Coastal_Command" title="RAF Coastal Command">RAF Coastal Command</a> during the Second World War, flying <a href="/wiki/Short_Sunderland" title="Short Sunderland">Sunderlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  In 1948, he moved to New York to work for IBM as a mathematical <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a>. In 1953, angered by Senator <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy" title="Joseph McCarthy">Joseph McCarthy</a>, Codd moved to <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa, Ontario</a>, Canada. In 1957 he returned to the US working for IBM and from 1961–1965 pursuing his doctorate in computer science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor</a>. Two years later he moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>, to work at IBM's <a href="/wiki/Almaden_Research_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Almaden Research Center">San Jose Research Laboratory</a>, where he continued to work until the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_Turing_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_Turing-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He was appointed <a href="/wiki/IBM_Fellow" title="IBM Fellow">IBM Fellow</a> in 1976. During the 1990s, his health deteriorated and he ceased work.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Codd received the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> in 1981,<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_Turing_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_Turing-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and in 1994 he was inducted as a <a href="/wiki/ACM_Fellow" title="ACM Fellow">Fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Codd died of heart failure at his home in Williams Island, Florida, at the age of 79 on 18 April 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-IBM_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBM-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edgar_F._Codd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Codd received a PhD in 1965 from the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor advised by <a href="/wiki/John_Henry_Holland" title="John Henry Holland">John Henry Holland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mathgene_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mathgene-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Independent_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-coddphd_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coddphd-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His thesis was about <a href="/wiki/Self-replication" title="Self-replication">self-replication</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automata" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellular automata">cellular automata</a>, extending on work of <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">von Neumann</a> and showing that a set of eight states was sufficient for <a href="/wiki/Universal_computation" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal computation">universal computation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_universal_constructor" title="Von Neumann universal constructor">construction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Codd%27s_cellular_automaton" title="Codd&#39;s cellular automaton">His design for a self-replicating computer</a> was only implemented in 2010.
</p><p>In the 1960s and 1970s he worked out his theories of data arrangement, issuing his paper "A Relational Model of Data for Large Shared Data Banks"<sup id="cite_ref-relationalmodel_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relationalmodel-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in 1970, after an internal IBM paper one year earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> To his disappointment, IBM proved slow to exploit his suggestions until commercial rivals started implementing them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Initially, IBM refused to implement the relational model to preserve revenue from <a href="/wiki/IMS/DB" class="mw-redirect" title="IMS/DB">IMS/DB</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Codd then showed IBM customers the potential of the implementation of its model, and they in turn pressured IBM. Then IBM included in its <a href="/wiki/IBM_Future_Systems_project" title="IBM Future Systems project">Future Systems</a> project a <a href="/wiki/IBM_System_R" title="IBM System R">System R</a> subproject – but put in charge of it developers who were not thoroughly familiar with Codd's ideas, and isolated the team from Codd.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As a result, they did not use Codd's own <a href="/wiki/Alpha_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpha (language)">Alpha</a> language but created a non-relational one, SEQUEL. Even so, SEQUEL was so superior to pre-relational systems that it was copied, in 1979, based on pre-launch papers presented at conferences, by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Ellison" title="Larry Ellison">Larry Ellison</a>, of Relational Software Inc, in his <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Database" title="Oracle Database">Oracle Database</a>, which actually reached market before <a href="/wiki/IBM_SQL/DS" title="IBM SQL/DS">SQL/DS</a> – because of the then-already proprietary status of the original name, SEQUEL had been renamed <a href="/wiki/SQL" title="SQL">SQL</a>.
</p><p>Codd continued to develop and extend his relational model, sometimes in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Christopher_J._Date" title="Christopher J. Date">Christopher J. Date</a>. One of the <a href="/wiki/Database_normalization" title="Database normalization">normalised forms</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Boyce%E2%80%93Codd_normal_form" title="Boyce–Codd normal form">Boyce–Codd normal form</a>, is named after him.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Codd%27s_theorem" title="Codd&#39;s theorem">Codd's theorem</a>, a result proven in his seminal work on the relational model, equates the expressive power of <a href="/wiki/Relational_algebra" title="Relational algebra">relational algebra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Relational_calculus" title="Relational calculus">relational calculus</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As the relational model started to become fashionable in the early 1980s, Codd fought a sometimes bitter campaign to prevent the term being misused by database vendors who had merely added a relational veneer to older technology. As part of this campaign, he published his <a href="/wiki/Codd%27s_12_rules" title="Codd&#39;s 12 rules">12 rules</a> to define what constituted a relational database. This made his position in IBM increasingly difficult, so he left to form his own consulting company with Chris Date and others.
</p><p>Codd coined the term <i><a href="/wiki/Online_analytical_processing" title="Online analytical processing">Online analytical processing (OLAP)</a></i> and wrote the "twelve laws of online analytical processing".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Controversy erupted, however, after it was discovered that this paper had been sponsored by Arbor Software (subsequently Hyperion, now acquired by Oracle), a conflict of interest that had not been disclosed, and <i><a href="/wiki/Computerworld" title="Computerworld">Computerworld</a></i> withdrew the paper.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Register_2007-01-26_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Register_2007-01-26-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/SIGMOD" title="SIGMOD">SIGMOD</a> renamed its highest prize to the <a href="/wiki/SIGMOD_Edgar_F._Codd_Innovations_Award" title="SIGMOD Edgar F. Codd Innovations Award">SIGMOD Edgar F. Codd Innovations Award</a>, in his honour.
</p>
