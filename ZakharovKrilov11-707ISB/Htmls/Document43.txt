<p><b>John D. Carmack II</b><sup id="cite_ref-wired.com_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (born August 20, 1970)<sup id="cite_ref-wired.com_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game developer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>. He co-founded the video game company <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a> and was the lead programmer of its games <i><a href="/wiki/Commander_Keen" title="Commander Keen">Commander Keen</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wolfenstein_3D" title="Wolfenstein 3D">Wolfenstein 3D</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i>, and their sequels. Carmack made innovations in <a href="/wiki/3D_computer_graphics" title="3D computer graphics">3D computer graphics</a>, such as his <a href="/wiki/Shadow_volume#Depth_fail" title="Shadow volume">Carmack's Reverse</a> algorithm for <a href="/wiki/Shadow_volume" title="Shadow volume">shadow volumes</a>. In 2013, he resigned from id to work full-time at <a href="/wiki/Oculus_VR" class="mw-redirect" title="Oculus VR">Oculus VR</a>, where he served as <a href="/wiki/Chief_technology_officer" title="Chief technology officer">CTO</a> and later Consulting CTO in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-consultant_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consultant-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carmack was born in <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Mission,_Kansas" title="Shawnee Mission, Kansas">Shawnee Mission, Kansas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-wired.com_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the son of local television news reporter Stan Carmack. He grew up in the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_metropolitan_area" title="Kansas City metropolitan area">Kansas City metropolitan area</a>, where he became interested in <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a> at an early age. He attended <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Mission_East_High_School" title="Shawnee Mission East High School">Shawnee Mission East High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prairie_Village,_Kansas" title="Prairie Village, Kansas">Prairie Village, Kansas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raytown_South_High_School" title="Raytown South High School">Raytown South High School</a> in nearby <a href="/wiki/Raytown,_Missouri" title="Raytown, Missouri">Raytown, Missouri</a>.
</p><p>Carmack was introduced to video games with the 1978 <a href="/wiki/Shoot_%27em_up" title="Shoot &#39;em up">shoot 'em up</a> title <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Invaders" title="Space Invaders">Space Invaders</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcades</a> during a summer vacation as a child. The 1980 <a href="/wiki/List_of_maze_video_games#Maze_chase_games" title="List of maze video games">maze chase</a> arcade game <i><a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a></i> also left a strong impression on him. He cited <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a> as the game developer he most admired.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As reported in David Kushner's <i><a href="/wiki/Masters_of_Doom" title="Masters of Doom">Masters of Doom</a></i>, when Carmack was 14, he broke into a school to help a group of children steal <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> computers. To gain entry to the building, Carmack concocted a sticky substance of <a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">thermite</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Vaseline" title="Vaseline">Vaseline</a> that melted through the windows. However, an overweight accomplice struggled to get through the hole and instead opened the window, setting off a silent alarm and alerting police. Carmack was arrested, and sent for psychiatric evaluation (the report mentions "no empathy for other human beings" and describes Carmack as "a brain on legs"). Carmack was then sentenced to a year in a juvenile home.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Missouri%E2%80%93Kansas_City" title="University of Missouri–Kansas City">University of Missouri–Kansas City</a> for two semesters before withdrawing to work as a freelance programmer.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Softdisk" title="Softdisk">Softdisk</a>, a computer company in <a href="/wiki/Shreveport,_Louisiana" title="Shreveport, Louisiana">Shreveport, Louisiana</a>, hired Carmack to work on <i><a href="/wiki/Softdisk_(disk_magazine)" title="Softdisk (disk magazine)">Softdisk G-S</a></i> (an <a href="/wiki/Apple_IIGS" title="Apple IIGS">Apple II<small>GS</small></a> publication), introducing him to <a href="/wiki/John_Romero" title="John Romero">John Romero</a> and other future key members of id Software such as <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Carmack" title="Adrian Carmack">Adrian Carmack</a> (not related). Later, Softdisk would place this team in charge of a new, but short-lived, bi-monthly game subscription product called  <i>Gamer's Edge</i> for the IBM PC (DOS) platform. In 1990, while still at Softdisk, Carmack, Romero, and others created the first of the <i><a href="/wiki/Commander_Keen" title="Commander Keen">Commander Keen</a></i> games, a series that was published by <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">Apogee Software</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/Shareware" title="Shareware">shareware</a> distribution model, from 1991 onwards. Afterwards, Carmack left Softdisk to co-found <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Carmack has pioneered or popularized the use of many techniques in computer graphics, including "<a href="/wiki/Adaptive_tile_refresh" title="Adaptive tile refresh">adaptive tile refresh</a>" for <i>Commander Keen</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ray_casting" title="Ray casting">ray casting</a> for <i>Hovertank 3-D</i>, <i>Catacomb 3-D</i>, and <i>Wolfenstein 3-D</i>, <a href="/wiki/Binary_space_partitioning" title="Binary space partitioning">binary space partitioning</a> which <i>Doom</i> became the first game to use, <a href="/wiki/Quake_engine#Speeding_up_the_rendering,_and_rendering_order" title="Quake engine">surface caching</a> which he invented for <i>Quake</i>, <a href="/wiki/Shadow_volume" title="Shadow volume">Carmack's Reverse</a> (formally known as z-fail stencil shadows) which he devised for <i>Doom 3</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Id_Tech_4#MegaTexture_rendering_technology" title="Id Tech 4">MegaTexture</a> technology, first used in <i><a href="/wiki/Enemy_Territory:_Quake_Wars" title="Enemy Territory: Quake Wars">Enemy Territory: Quake Wars</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Carmack's engines have also been licensed for use in other influential first-person shooters such as <i><a href="/wiki/Half-Life_(video_game)" title="Half-Life (video game)">Half-Life</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Call_of_Duty" title="Call of Duty">Call of Duty</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor_(1999_video_game)" title="Medal of Honor (1999 video game)">Medal of Honor</a></i>. In 2007, when Carmack was on vacation with his wife, he ended up playing some games on his cellphone, and decided he was going to make a "good" mobile game.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On August 7, 2013, Carmack joined <a href="/wiki/Oculus_Rift" title="Oculus Rift">Oculus VR</a> as their <a href="/wiki/Chief_technology_officer" title="Chief technology officer">CTO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On November 22, 2013, he resigned from <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a> to work full-time at Oculus VR.<sup id="cite_ref-polygon_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-polygon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Carmack's reason for leaving was because id's parent company <a href="/wiki/ZeniMax_Media" title="ZeniMax Media">ZeniMax Media</a> didn't want to support Oculus Rift.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Carmack's role at both companies later became central to a ZeniMax <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> against Oculus' parent company, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, claiming that Oculus stole ZeniMax's virtual reality <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The trial jury absolved Carmack of liability, though Oculus and other corporate officers were held liable for trademark, copyright, and contract violations.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg/170px-John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg/255px-John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg/340px-John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1014" data-file-height="1283" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_-_The_Dawn_of_Mobile_VR_-_Game_Developer_Conference_2015_-_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carmack speaking about “The Dawn of Mobile VR” during the <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">Game Developers Conference</a> 2015</div></div></div>
<p>In February 2017, Carmack sued ZeniMax, claiming the company had refused to pay him the remaining $22.5 million owed him from their purchase of id Software.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> By October 2018, Carmack stated that he and ZeniMax reached an agreement and that "Zenimax has fully satisfied their obligations to me", ending the suit.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 13, 2019, Carmack announced that he is stepping down from the Oculus CTO role to become a "Consulting CTO" in order to allocate more time to his work on <a href="/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence" title="Artificial general intelligence">artificial general intelligence</a> (AGI).<sup id="cite_ref-consultant_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-consultant-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Armadillo_Aerospace">Armadillo Aerospace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Armadillo Aerospace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Armadillo_Aerospace" title="Armadillo Aerospace">Armadillo Aerospace</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo,_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9,_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg/170px-John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg/255px-John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg/340px-John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo%2C_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9%2C_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="426" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_during_the_X-Prize_Cup_2005_in_Las_Cruces_and_Alamogordo,_New_Mexico_--_October_6-9,_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carmack during the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Ansari_X_PRIZE" class="mw-redirect" title="Ansari X PRIZE">X PRIZE</a> Cup in <a href="/wiki/Las_Cruces,_New_Mexico" title="Las Cruces, New Mexico">Las Cruces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alamogordo,_New_Mexico" title="Alamogordo, New Mexico">Alamogordo, New Mexico</a></div></div></div>
<p>Around 2000, Carmack became interested in rocketry, a hobby of his youth. Reviewing how much money he was spending on customizing Ferraris,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Carmack realized he could do significant work in hobby aerospace. He began by giving financial support to a few local amateur engineers. Carmack funded the company out of his own pocket, for "something north of a million dollars a year."<sup id="cite_ref-nsj20130801_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nsj20130801-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The company of hobbyists made steady progress toward their goals of suborbital space flight and eventual orbital vehicles. In October 2008, <a href="/wiki/Armadillo_Aerospace" title="Armadillo Aerospace">Armadillo Aerospace</a> competed in a NASA contest known as the Lunar Lander Challenge, winning first place in the Level 1 competition along with $350,000. In September 2009, they completed Level 2 and were awarded $500,000.<sup id="cite_ref-d0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-d0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Armadillo_Aerospace_Claim_Level_2_NGLLC_Prize_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Armadillo_Aerospace_Claim_Level_2_NGLLC_Prize-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lunar_lander_qualifies_for_prize_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lunar_lander_qualifies_for_prize-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The company went into "hibernation mode" in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-nsj20130801_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nsj20130801-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-source_software">Open-source software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Open-source software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carmack is a well-known advocate of <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a>, and has repeatedly voiced his opposition to <a href="/wiki/Software_patent" title="Software patent">software patents</a>, equating them to robbery.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  He has also contributed to open-source projects, such as starting the initial port of the <a href="/wiki/X_Window_System" title="X Window System">X Window System</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X_Server" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X Server">Mac OS X Server</a> and working to improve the OpenGL drivers for Linux through the <a href="/wiki/Utah_GLX" title="Utah GLX">Utah GLX</a> project.
</p><p>Carmack released the source code for <i>Wolfenstein 3D</i> in 1995 and the <i>Doom</i> source code in 1997. When the source code to <i>Quake</i> was leaked and circulated among the <i>Quake</i> community underground in 1996, a programmer unaffiliated with id Software used it to <a href="/wiki/Linux_Quake_port" class="mw-redirect" title="Linux Quake port">port <i>Quake</i> to Linux</a>, and subsequently sent the patches to Carmack. Instead of pursuing legal action, id Software, at Carmack's behest, used the patches as the foundation for a company-sanctioned <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> port.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> id Software has since publicly released the source code to <i>Quake</i>, <i>Quake 2</i>, <i>Quake 3</i> and lastly <i>Doom 3</i> (and later the <i>BFG Edition</i>), all under the <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a> (GPL). The <i>Doom</i> source code was also re-released under the GPL in 1999. The <a href="/wiki/Id_Tech_4" title="Id Tech 4">id Tech 4</a> engine, more commonly known as the "<a href="/wiki/Doom_3" title="Doom 3">Doom 3</a> engine", has also been released as <a href="/wiki/Open-source_license" title="Open-source license">open-source license</a> under the GPL.<sup id="cite_ref-Doom_3_GPL_Source_Release_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doom_3_GPL_Source_Release-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The source code for <i><a href="/wiki/Hovertank_3D" title="Hovertank 3D">Hovertank 3D</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Catacomb_3D" class="mw-redirect" title="Catacomb 3D">Catacomb 3D</a></i> (as well as Carmack's earlier <i><a href="/wiki/Catacomb_(video_game)" title="Catacomb (video game)">Catacomb</a></i>) was released in June 2014 by <a href="/wiki/Flat_Rock_Software" class="mw-redirect" title="Flat Rock Software">Flat Rock Software</a> with Carmack's blessing.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, Carmack has several times over the years voiced a skeptical opinion about Linux as a gaming platform;<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> for instance in 2013 he argued for <a href="/wiki/Emulation_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Emulation (computing)">emulation</a> <i>"as proper technical direction for gaming on linux"</i><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and in 2014 he voiced the opinion that Linux might be the biggest problem for the success of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_Machine_(hardware_platform)" title="Steam Machine (hardware platform)">Steam Machine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carmack contributes to charities and gaming communities. Some of the recipients of Carmack's <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization">charitable</a> contributions include his former high school, promoters of open-source software, opponents of software patents, and game enthusiasts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Carmack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_(Thresh),_May_1997.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg/220px-John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg/330px-John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg/440px-John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_%28Thresh%29%2C_May_1997.jpg 2x" data-file-width="777" data-file-height="605" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Carmack_%26_Dennis_Fong_(Thresh),_May_1997.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carmack &amp; <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Fong" title="Dennis Fong">Dennis Fong</a> (Thresh), May 1997</div></div></div>
<p>Carmack was so successful at id that by mid-1994 he had purchased 2 Ferraris: a 328 and a <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_Testarossa" title="Ferrari Testarossa">Ferrari Testarossa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lombardi199407_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lombardi199407-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 1997, he gave away one of his <a href="/wiki/Ferrari" title="Ferrari">Ferraris</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_328_GTS" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrari 328 GTS">328</a> model) as a prize to <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Fong" title="Dennis Fong">Dennis Fong</a>, the winner of the <i>Quake</i> tournament "<a href="/wiki/Red_Annihilation" title="Red Annihilation">Red Annihilation</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He met his wife, <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Anna_Kang" title="Katherine Anna Kang">Katherine Anna Kang</a>, at the 1997 <a href="/wiki/QuakeCon" title="QuakeCon">QuakeCon</a> when she visited id's offices. As a bet, Kang challenged Carmack to sponsor the first All Female Quake Tournament if she was able to produce a significant number of participants. Carmack predicted a maximum of 25 participants, but there were 1,500.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Carmack and Kang married on January 1, 2000, and planned a ceremony in Hawaii. Steve Jobs requested that they would postpone the ceremony so he could attend the MacWorld Expo on January 5, 2000. Carmack declined and suggested making a video instead.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Carmack and his wife had a son in 2004. Carmack has a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> last updated in 2006 (previously a <a href="/wiki/Finger_protocol" title="Finger protocol">.plan</a>), an active <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account, and also occasionally posts comments to <a href="/wiki/Slashdot" title="Slashdot">Slashdot</a>.
</p><p>As a game developer, Carmack differed from many of his contemporaries by avoiding commitment to a final release date for any game he was developing. Instead, when asked for a release date on a new title, Carmack would usually reply that the game would be released "when it's done.".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In a 2019, as a guest on the Joe Rogan podcast Carmack stated that his beliefs have changed over time. "I largely recant from that now." On <a href="/wiki/Rage_(video_game)" title="Rage (video game)">Rage</a>'s 6-year development time he says "I think we should have done whatever it would have taken to ship it 2 years earlier". Carmack also reflects on the internal development of Quake in this regard and describes it as "traumatic" and says Id Software could have split the game into two parts and shipped it earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Employees at <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">Apogee</a>, in their past years the publishers of games by id Software, adopted this business practice as well.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carmack supported the <a href="/wiki/Ron_Paul_2012_presidential_campaign" title="Ron Paul 2012 presidential campaign">2012 presidential campaign</a> of <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_Party_(United_States)" title="Libertarian Party (United States)">Libertarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ron_Paul" title="Ron Paul">Ron Paul</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carmack is an <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During his time at id Software, a medium pepperoni pizza would arrive for Carmack from <a href="/wiki/Domino%27s_Pizza" title="Domino&#39;s Pizza">Domino's Pizza</a> almost every day, carried by the same delivery person for more than 15 years. Carmack had been such a regular customer that they continued to charge him 1995 prices.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On occasion he has commended the efforts of similarly focused programmers&#160;– first <a href="/wiki/Ken_Silverman" title="Ken Silverman">Ken Silverman</a>, who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Build_(game_engine)" title="Build (game engine)">Build engine</a> for <a href="/wiki/3D_Realms" title="3D Realms">3D Realms</a>, and later with <a href="/wiki/Tim_Sweeney_(game_developer)" title="Tim Sweeney (game developer)">Tim Sweeney</a> of <a href="/wiki/Epic_Games" title="Epic Games">Epic Games</a>, who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Unreal_Engine" title="Unreal Engine">Unreal Engine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
