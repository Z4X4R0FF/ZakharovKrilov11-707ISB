<p><b>Paul Graham</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 13 November 1964)<sup id="cite_ref-DOB_LibCongress_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOB_LibCongress-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a>-born American <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_essayists" title="List of essayists">essayist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capitalist</a>, and author. He is best known for his work on the <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> <a href="/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language)" title="Lisp (programming language)">Lisp</a>, his former startup <a href="/wiki/Viaweb" title="Viaweb">Viaweb</a> (later renamed <i>Yahoo! Store</i>), cofounding the influential startup accelerator and seed capital firm <a href="/wiki/Y_Combinator" title="Y Combinator">Y Combinator</a>, his blog, and <a href="/wiki/Hacker_News" title="Hacker News">Hacker News</a>. He is the author of several <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a> books, including: <i><a href="/wiki/On_Lisp" title="On Lisp">On Lisp</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-onlisp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-onlisp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (1993), <i>ANSI Common Lisp</i><sup id="cite_ref-ansi_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ansi-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (1995), and <i><a href="/wiki/Hackers_%26_Painters" title="Hackers &amp; Painters">Hackers &amp; Painters</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-hackersandpainters_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hackersandpainters-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (2004). Technology journalist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Levy" title="Steven Levy">Steven Levy</a> has described Graham as a "hacker philosopher".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graham and his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in 1968, where he later attended <a href="/wiki/Gateway_High_School_(Pennsylvania)" title="Gateway High School (Pennsylvania)">Gateway High School</a>. Graham gained interest in science and mathematics from his father who was a nuclear physicist.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graham received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a><sup id="cite_ref-bio_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> (1986).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He then attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, earning <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> (1988) and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> (1990) degrees in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bio_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SpeakerMix_bio_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpeakerMix_bio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He has also studied <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_School_of_Design" title="Rhode Island School of Design">Rhode Island School of Design</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_di_Belle_Arti_Firenze" class="mw-redirect" title="Accademia di Belle Arti Firenze">Accademia di Belle Arti</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bio_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SpeakerMix_bio_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpeakerMix_bio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, Graham and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Tappan_Morris" title="Robert Tappan Morris">Robert Morris</a> founded <a href="/wiki/Viaweb" title="Viaweb">Viaweb</a> and recruited <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Blackwell" title="Trevor Blackwell">Trevor Blackwell</a> shortly after. <a href="/wiki/Viaweb" title="Viaweb">Viaweb</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/Application_service_provider" title="Application service provider">application service provider</a> (ASP) according to Graham. Viaweb's software,  written mostly in <a href="/wiki/Common_Lisp" title="Common Lisp">Common Lisp</a>, allowed users to make their own <a href="/wiki/Internet_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet store">Internet stores</a>. In the summer of 1998, Viaweb was sold to <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> for 455,000 shares of Yahoo! stock, valued at $49.6 million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> After the acquisition, the product became <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Store" class="mw-redirect" title="Yahoo! Store">Yahoo! Store</a>.
</p><p>Graham later gained notice for his essays, which he posts to his personal website. Essay subjects range from "Beating the Averages",<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> which compares Lisp to other <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> and introduced the hypothetical programming language <a href="#The_Blub_paradox">Blub</a>, to "Why Nerds are Unpopular",<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> a discussion of nerd life in high school. A collection of his essays has been published as <i><a href="/wiki/Hackers_%26_Painters" title="Hackers &amp; Painters">Hackers &amp; Painters</a></i> <sup id="cite_ref-hackersandpainters_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hackersandpainters-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/O%27Reilly_Media" title="O&#39;Reilly Media">O'Reilly Media</a>, which includes a discussion of the growth of Viaweb and what Graham perceives to be the advantages of Lisp to program it.
</p><p>In 2001, Graham announced that he was working on a new <a href="/wiki/Dialect_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialect (computing)">dialect</a> of Lisp named <a href="/wiki/Arc_(programming_language)" title="Arc (programming language)">Arc</a>. It was released on 29 January 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Over the years since, he has written several essays describing features or goals of the language, and some internal projects at Y Combinator have been written in Arc, most notably the Hacker News web forum and news aggregator program.
</p><p>In October 2019, Graham announced a <a href="/wiki/Technical_specification" class="mw-redirect" title="Technical specification">specification</a> for another new dialect of Lisp, written in itself, named Bel.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, after giving a talk at the Harvard Computer Society later published as "How to Start a Startup", Graham along with <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Blackwell" title="Trevor Blackwell">Trevor Blackwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Livingston" title="Jessica Livingston">Jessica Livingston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Tappan_Morris" title="Robert Tappan Morris">Robert Morris</a> started <a href="/wiki/Y_Combinator" title="Y Combinator">Y Combinator</a> to provide <a href="/wiki/Seed_funding" class="mw-redirect" title="Seed funding">seed funding</a> to a large number of <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startups</a>, particularly those started by younger, more technically oriented founders. Y Combinator has now invested in more than 1300 startups, including <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">Reddit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justin.tv" title="Justin.tv">Justin.tv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xobni" title="Xobni">Xobni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dropbox_(service)" title="Dropbox (service)">Dropbox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airbnb" title="Airbnb">Airbnb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stripe_(company)" title="Stripe (company)">Stripe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/BusinessWeek" class="mw-redirect" title="BusinessWeek">BusinessWeek</a></i> included Paul Graham in 2008 edition of its annual feature, <i>The 25 Most Influential People on the Web</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to the proposed <a href="/wiki/Stop_Online_Piracy_Act" title="Stop Online Piracy Act">Stop Online Piracy Act</a> (SOPA), Graham announced in late 2011 that no representatives of any company supporting it would be invited to Y Combinator's Demo Day events.<sup id="cite_ref-Demo_Day_SOPA_cutoff_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Demo_Day_SOPA_cutoff-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2014, Graham stepped down from his day-to-day role at Y Combinator.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span id="Graham.27s_hierarchy_of_disagreement"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Graham's_hierarchy_of_disagreement"><span id="Graham&#39;s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement"></span> Graham's hierarchy of disagreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Graham&#039;s hierarchy of disagreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg/400px-Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg/600px-Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg/800px-Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="707" data-file-height="530" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Graham%27s_Hierarchy_of_Disagreement-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graham's hierarchy of disagreement</div></div></div>
<p>Graham proposed a "disagreement hierarchy" in a 2008 essay "How to Disagree",<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> putting types of <a href="/wiki/Argument" title="Argument">argument</a> into a seven-point hierarchy and observing that "If moving up the disagreement hierarchy makes people less mean, that will make most of them happier." Graham also suggested that the hierarchy can be thought of as a pyramid, as the highest forms of disagreement are rarer.
</p><p>Following this hierarchy, Graham notes that articulate forms of name-calling (e.g., "The author is a self-important dilettante") are no different from crude insults.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Blub_paradox"><span id="Blub"></span> The Blub paradox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Graham_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Blub paradox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
Graham considers the hierarchy of <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> with the example of "Blub", a hypothetically average language "right in the middle of the abstractness continuum. It is not the most powerful language, but it is more powerful than <a href="/wiki/COBOL" title="COBOL">Cobol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Machine_code" title="Machine code">machine language</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It was used by Graham to illustrate a comparison, beyond <a href="/wiki/Turing_completeness" title="Turing completeness">Turing completeness</a>, of programming language power, and more specifically to illustrate the difficulty of comparing a programming language one knows to one that one does not.<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...These studies would like to formally prove that a certain language is more or less expressive than another language. Determining such a relation between languages objectively rather than subjectively seems to be somewhat problematic, a phenomenon that Paul Graham has discussed in "The Blub Paradox".<sup id="cite_ref-blub1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blub1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-blub2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blub2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Graham considers a hypothetical Blub programmer. When the programmer looks down the "power continuum", he considers the lower languages to be less powerful because they miss some feature that a Blub programmer is used to. But when he looks up, he fails to realise that he is looking up: he merely sees "weird languages" with unnecessary features and assumes they are equivalent in power, but with "other hairy stuff thrown in as well". When Graham considers the point of view of a programmer using a language higher than Blub, he describes that programmer as looking down on Blub and noting its "missing" features from the point of view of the higher language.<sup id="cite_ref-blub1_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blub1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Graham describes this as the "Blub paradox" and concludes that "By induction, the only programmers in a position to see all the differences in power between the various languages are those who understand the most powerful one."<sup id="cite_ref-blub1_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blub1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept has been cited by programmers such as <a href="/wiki/Joel_Spolsky" title="Joel Spolsky">Joel Spolsky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
