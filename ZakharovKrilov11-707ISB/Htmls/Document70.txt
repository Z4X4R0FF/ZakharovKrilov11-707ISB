<p><b>Brendan Eich</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born July 4, 1961)<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1997_09_09_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1997_09_09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American technologist and creator of the <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a>. He co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> project,<sup id="cite_ref-Kim1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation" title="Mozilla Foundation">Mozilla Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Corporation" title="Mozilla Corporation">Mozilla Corporation</a>, and served as the Mozilla Corporation's <a href="/wiki/Chief_technical_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief technical officer">chief technical officer</a> and, briefly, as its <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He is the CEO of <a href="/wiki/Brave_(web_browser)" title="Brave (web browser)">Brave Software</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnet.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnet.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brendan_Eich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eich grew up in Pittsburgh; Gaithersburg, Maryland; and <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto" class="mw-redirect" title="Palo Alto">Palo Alto</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1997_09_09_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1997_09_09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and he attended <a href="/wiki/Ellwood_P._Cubberley_High_School" title="Ellwood P. Cubberley High School">Ellwood P. Cubberley High School</a>, graduating in the class of 1979. He received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a> in mathematics and computer science at <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara_University" title="Santa Clara University">Santa Clara University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1997_09_09_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1997_09_09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and he received his <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master&#39;s degree">master's degree</a> in 1985 from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1997_09_09_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1997_09_09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He began his career at <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">Silicon Graphics</a>, working for seven years on <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> and network code.<sup id="cite_ref-aboutcom_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutcom-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He then worked for three years at <a href="/wiki/MicroUnity" title="MicroUnity">MicroUnity Systems Engineering</a> writing <a href="/wiki/Kernel_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kernel (computer science)">microkernel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">DSP</a> code.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_and_JavaScript">Netscape and JavaScript</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brendan_Eich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Netscape and JavaScript">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eich started work at <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Communications_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Netscape Communications Corporation">Netscape Communications Corporation</a> in April 1995. Eich originally joined intending to put <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language)" title="Scheme (programming language)">Scheme</a> "in the browser",<sup id="cite_ref-Saternos2014_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saternos2014-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but his Netscape superiors insisted that the language's <a href="/wiki/Syntax_(programming_languages)" title="Syntax (programming languages)">syntax</a> resemble that of <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a>. As a result, Eich devised a language that had much of the functionality of Scheme, the <a href="/wiki/Object-oriented_programming" title="Object-oriented programming">object-orientation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Self_(programming_language)" title="Self (programming language)">Self</a>, and the syntax of Java. He completed the first version in ten days in order to accommodate the <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator_2" title="Netscape Navigator 2">Navigator 2.0</a> Beta release schedule,<sup id="cite_ref-Saternos2014_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saternos2014-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Severance_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Severance-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and was called Mocha, but renamed LiveScript in September 1995 and finally named <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> in December.<sup id="cite_ref-quirks_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quirks-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Eich continued to oversee the development of <a href="/wiki/SpiderMonkey_(JavaScript_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="SpiderMonkey (JavaScript engine)">SpiderMonkey</a>, the specific implementation of JavaScript in Navigator.<sup id="cite_ref-BE201106_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BE201106-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mozilla">Mozilla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brendan_Eich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mozilla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early 1998, Eich co-founded the free and open source software project <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Zawinski" title="Jamie Zawinski">Jamie Zawinski</a> and others, creating the mozilla.org website, which was meant to manage open-source contributions to the Netscape source code. He served as Mozilla's chief <a href="/wiki/Systems_architect" title="Systems architect">architect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Seibel2009_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seibel2009-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">AOL</a> bought Netscape in 1999. After AOL shut down the Netscape browser unit in July 2003, Eich helped spin out the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation" title="Mozilla Foundation">Mozilla Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mozilla-press-2005-08-03_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mozilla-press-2005-08-03-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2005, after serving as a lead technologist and as a member of the board of directors of the Mozilla Foundation, Eich became <a href="/wiki/Chief_technical_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief technical officer">chief technical officer</a> (CTO) of the newly founded <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Corporation" title="Mozilla Corporation">Mozilla Corporation</a>, meant to be the Mozilla Foundation's for-profit arm.<sup id="cite_ref-mozilla-press-2005-08-03_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mozilla-press-2005-08-03-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Eich continued to "own" the Mozilla SpiderMonkey module, its JavaScript engine, until he passed on the ownership of it in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-BE201106_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BE201106-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Appointment_to_CEO.2C_controversy_and_resignation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment_to_CEO,_controversy_and_resignation">Appointment to CEO, controversy and resignation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brendan_Eich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appointment to CEO, controversy and resignation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 24, 2014, Mozilla made the decision to appoint Eich as CEO of Mozilla Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-ArsTechnicaMozillaEmployees_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArsTechnicaMozillaEmployees-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_Resignation_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ_Resignation-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The appointment triggered widespread criticism due to Eich's past political donations<sup id="cite_ref-ArsTechnicaMozillaEmployees_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArsTechnicaMozillaEmployees-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inc_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inc-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> – specifically, a 2008 donation of $1,000 to <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_8_(2008)" class="mw-redirect" title="California Proposition 8 (2008)">California Proposition 8</a>, which called for the banning of <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_California" title="Same-sex marriage in California">same-sex marriage in California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pinknews_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pinknews-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and donations in the amount of $2,100 to Proposition 8 supporter <a href="/wiki/Tom_McClintock" title="Tom McClintock">Tom McClintock</a> between 2008 and 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-sorrow_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sorrow-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> initially reported that, in protest against his coming appointment, half of Mozilla's board (<a href="/wiki/Gary_Kovacs" title="Gary Kovacs">Gary Kovacs</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lilly_(computer_scientist)" title="John Lilly (computer scientist)">John Lilly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Siminoff" title="Ellen Siminoff">Ellen Siminoff</a>) stepped down,<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> leaving <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Baker" title="Mitchell Baker">Mitchell Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reid_Hoffman" title="Reid Hoffman">Reid Hoffman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Katharina_Borchert" title="Katharina Borchert">Katharina Borchert</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-shankland_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shankland-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CNET" title="CNET">CNET</a> later reported that of the three board members who had left, only Lilly left due to Eich's appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-shankland_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shankland-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Lilly told <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, "I left rather than appoint him", and declined to elaborate further.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 26, 2014, Eich expressed "sorrow for causing pain" and pledged to "work with LGBT communities and allies" at Mozilla.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sorrow_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sorrow-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Some of the activists created an online campaign against Eich, with online dating site <a href="/wiki/OkCupid" title="OkCupid">OkCupid</a> automatically displaying a message to Firefox users with information about Eich's donation, and suggesting that users switch to a different browser (although giving them a link to continue with Firefox).<sup id="cite_ref-latimes-donation_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-donation-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CREDO_Mobile" title="CREDO Mobile">CREDO Mobile</a> collected more than 50,000 signatures demanding that Eich resign.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_Resignation_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ_Resignation-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 11 days as CEO, Eich resigned on April 3, 2014, and left Mozilla over his opposition to same-sex marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-standard_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-standard-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In his personal blog, he posted, "under the present circumstances, I cannot be an effective leader".<sup id="cite_ref-Kim1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eichblog_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eichblog-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Mozilla made a press release saying that board members tried to get Eich to stay in the company in a different role, but that he had chosen to sever ties for the time being.<sup id="cite_ref-TheRegister_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheRegister-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brave_Software">Brave Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brendan_Eich&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Brave Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eich is the CEO of Brave Software, an Internet browser platform company that raised $2.5 million in early funding from <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnet.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnet.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In January 2016, the company released developer versions of its open-source, <a href="/wiki/Chromium_(web_browser)" title="Chromium (web browser)">Chromium</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Brave_(web_browser)" title="Brave (web browser)">Brave web browser</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> which blocks <a href="/wiki/Online_advertising" title="Online advertising">ads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_tracking" title="Web tracking">trackers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At Brave Software, Eich co-created the Basic Attention Token (BAT), a <a href="/wiki/Cryptocurrency" title="Cryptocurrency">cryptocurrency</a> designed for use in the Brave browser. BAT launched its <a href="/wiki/Initial_coin_offering" title="Initial coin offering">ICO</a> on May 31, 2017, and raised $35 million.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, during the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a>, Eich ardently denied the effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Face_masks_during_the_COVID-19_pandemic" title="Face masks during the COVID-19 pandemic">face masks</a> and <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_lockdowns" title="COVID-19 lockdowns">lockdowns</a> in combating the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_of_COVID-19" title="Transmission of COVID-19">transmission of COVID-19</a> and stated of the United States' top <a href="/wiki/Immunologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Immunologist">immunologist</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Anthony_Fauci" title="Anthony Fauci">[Anthony] Fauci</a> lies a lot", sparking calls for his removal from Brave on social media.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
