<p><b>Brad Cox</b> (May 2, 1944 – January 2, 2021)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> who was known mostly for creating the <a href="/wiki/Objective-C" title="Objective-C">Objective-C</a> <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> with his business partner Tom Love and for his work in <a href="/wiki/Software_engineering" title="Software engineering">software engineering</a> (specifically <a href="/wiki/Software_reuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Software reuse">software reuse</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Software_componentry" class="mw-redirect" title="Software componentry">software componentry</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brad_Cox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cox received his Bachelor of Science Degree in <a href="/wiki/Organic_Chemistry" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic Chemistry">Organic Chemistry</a> and Mathematics from <a href="/wiki/Furman_University" title="Furman University">Furman University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
and his Ph.D. from the Department of Mathematical Biology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Among his first known software projects, he wrote a <a href="/wiki/PDP-8" title="PDP-8">PDP-8</a> program for simulating clusters of neurons.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He worked at the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Oceanographic_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute">Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute</a> before moving into the software profession.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Cox invented his own programming language, <a href="/wiki/Objective-C" title="Objective-C">Objective-C</a>, which he used in his early career, he has stated in an interview for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Masterminds_of_programming&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Masterminds of programming (page does not exist)">Masterminds of programming</a> book that he isn't interested in <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> but rather in <a href="/wiki/Software_component" class="mw-redirect" title="Software component">software components</a>, and he regards languages as mere tools for building and combining parts of software.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cox was also an <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneur</a>, having founded the <a href="/wiki/Stepstone" title="Stepstone">Stepstone</a> company together with Tom Love for releasing the first Objective-C implementation. Later, <a href="/wiki/NeXT" title="NeXT">NeXT</a> acquired Objective-C from Stepstone. Objective-C continued to be the primary programming language for writing software for Apple's <a href="/wiki/OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="OS X">OS X</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
