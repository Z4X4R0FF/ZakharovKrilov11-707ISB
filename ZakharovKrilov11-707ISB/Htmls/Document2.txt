	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >Tarn Adams</h1>

<p><b>Tarn Adams</b> (born April 17, 1978) is an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_game_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game programmer">computer game programmer</a>, best known for his work on <i><a href="/wiki/Dwarf_Fortress" title="Dwarf Fortress">Dwarf Fortress</a>.</i> He has been working on the game since 2002 with his older brother Zach. He learned programming in his childhood, and has been designing computer games as a hobby. He quit his first year of a <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> post doctorate at <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas A&amp;M">Texas A&amp;M</a> to focus on <a href="/wiki/Video_game_development" title="Video game development">game development</a> in 2006.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tarn_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tarn was born in <a href="/wiki/Silverdale,_Washington" title="Silverdale, Washington">Silverdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/US" class="mw-redirect" title="US">US</a>,  in 1978. His father, Dan, worked at a <a href="/wiki/Waste_water_treatment_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Waste water treatment plant">waste water treatment plant</a> and used to manage data. He credited his father for teaching him to code and his closeness to his brother, Zach, to their family's constant shifting due to their father's work. Tarn and Zach grew up playing computer games and, with notebooks in hand, drawing their own renditions of the randomly generated creatures they encountered and logging their journeys in detail. In fifth grade, Tarn wrote his first animation game with Zach. Explaining his reluctance to socialize,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> he said, "I was a get-home-from-school, get-on-the-computer kind of kid." Tarn stated that the main reason they started writing games was to be able to play them themselves, and complicated and unpredictable behavior guaranteed <a href="/wiki/Replayability" class="mw-redirect" title="Replayability">replayability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_gamer_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_gamer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In high school Tarn and Zach created a spacecraft game that simulated sections of a rocket blowing off and released their first publicly available game on <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">America Online</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Escapist_article_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escapist_article-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In sixth grade, they started their first <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Fantasy game">fantasy game</a> called <i>dragslay</i> written in <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a>. It consisted of single battles leading to a final one with a dragon. A few years later, Tarn rewrote it in the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> programming language, and it featured minute details and kept track of populations of units in the generated world.<sup id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
After <i>dragslay</i>, Tarn and Zach started working on another adventure game, focusing on world generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_video_game" title="Role-playing video game">role-playing video game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ultima_(series)" title="Ultima (series)">Ultima</a></i> inspired them regarding world development. For four years, after working on the adventure title and rendering it in <a href="/wiki/3D_graphics" class="mw-redirect" title="3D graphics">3D graphics</a>, they completed <i>Slaves to Armok: God of Blood</i>. "Armok" was the name of the game's deity from the <a href="/wiki/Variable_(computer_science)" title="Variable (computer science)">variable</a> "arm_ok", which was used in <i>dragslay</i> to indicate how many arms were left on a particular unit. The random story generator was originated by both of them writing stories.<sup id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Tarn said, "you could zoom in on your character, and it’d tell you how curly his leg hairs were, and the melting and flash points of various materials, It was insane."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The brothers posted it on their website in 2000, but by 2004 the project started to face increasing problems. Tarn announced in 2004 on his forums that he was going to shift his main project from <i>Armok</i> to a side project called <i>Dwarf Fortress</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_gamer_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_gamer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ZachAdams.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/ZachAdams.jpg/150px-ZachAdams.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/ZachAdams.jpg/225px-ZachAdams.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/ZachAdams.jpg/300px-ZachAdams.jpg 2x" data-file-width="598" data-file-height="757" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ZachAdams.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Adams' older brother, Zach, in 2013</div></div></div><p>Tarn earned a degree in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He applied for his doctorate at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, completing it in 2005 with a dissertation titled "Flat Chains in <a href="/wiki/Banach_Space" class="mw-redirect" title="Banach Space">Banach Spaces</a>", which was published in <i>The Journal of Geometric Analysis</i>. During his first year at Stanford, he said he was under pressure, that the professional environment and competitiveness affected him negatively. He cited the conflict between studying mathematics and developing video games as the reason. This stressful situation left him depressed and he admitted to having a brief encounter with drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shift_to_game_development">Shift to game development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tarn_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shift to game development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tarn and Zach started a company called Bay 12 Games, where they posted <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> games and attracted a small following. Tarn assumed the name "Toady One" and Zach "ThreeToe". Tarn's background in mathematics helped in the development of <a href="/wiki/Algorithms" class="mw-redirect" title="Algorithms">algorithms</a> with <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">spatial</a> considerations. With his skill in programming and Zach's background in ancient history and storytelling, together the brothers designed and developed various projects.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_gamer_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_gamer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They worked on some other small projects during graduation which were released on their website. Tarn put up a <a href="/wiki/PayPal" title="PayPal">PayPal</a> button after a request from a fan; similarly, a subscriber system was added later. In the next five months, they made around $300, which brought in only enough to cover their site's $20 hosting cost.<sup id="cite_ref-Escapist_article_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escapist_article-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They made side projects like <i>Corin</i> and <i>Kobold Quest</i> in a few days and <i>Squiggles</i> was made in three hours. Other games such as <i>Liberal Crime Squad</i> and <i>WWI Medic</i>, were also developed.<sup id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Tarn started his post doctorate in <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas A&amp;M">Texas A&amp;M</a>, which was his goal since his undergraduate days. He decided to leave during the first year due to the increasingly stressful situation<sup id="cite_ref-PC_gamer_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_gamer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and is said to have broken down in the head of department's office.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> After being offered to stay another year and a $50,000 stipend, he agreed and eventually left to devote his full attention to developing <i>Dwarf Fortress</i> and other games, which was until then only a hobby. He said, "At the end of a math problem, you have a paper and maybe you publish it, and the paper can be a building block for the edifice of mathematics, but to me that’s not so important. But working on a problem and having a game when you’re done? That’s pretty damn cool."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarf_Fortress"><i>Dwarf Fortress</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tarn_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dwarf Fortress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_Fortress" title="Dwarf Fortress">Dwarf Fortress</a></div>
<p>After quitting university, Tarn thought he would use up his savings after a year ($15,000) and need a job to sustain himself. <i>Dwarf Fortress</i> was originally started in October 2002 as a two-month side project, but was suspended soon after in preference over <i>Armok's</i> development. He had developed a game called <i>Mutant Miner</i>. It was a <a href="/wiki/Turn-based_gaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Turn-based gaming">turn-based</a> game where players look for minerals and dig out tunnels while dealing with threats. He realized the need to be able to manage many miners and not only have a high score list, but also store more minute details, which was the beginning of the project.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The game's primary mode is set in a <a href="/wiki/Procedural_generation" title="Procedural generation">procedurally generated</a> fantasy world in which the player indirectly controls a group of <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Middle-earth)" title="Dwarf (Middle-earth)">dwarves</a> and attempts to construct a successful and wealthy underground fortress.<sup id="cite_ref-RPSGillen_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSGillen-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Its development continued till 8 August 2006, when the first <a href="/wiki/Alpha_version" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpha version">alpha version</a> was released. Donations reached $800–$1000 in the following months, this average increased gradually until Tarn and Zach were financially stable.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_gamer_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_gamer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They then decided to solely rely on donations. The game eventually attracted a cult following and various web communities devoted to it were formed. <a href="/wiki/Video_game_journalism" title="Video game journalism">Videogame journalists</a> later covered the game. They focused on it being a two-member project surviving through donations. Critics praised its complex, <a href="/wiki/Emergent_gameplay" title="Emergent gameplay">emergent gameplay</a> but had mixed reactions to its difficulty.<sup id="cite_ref-ATJohnston_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATJohnston-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IGNSpencer2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNSpencer2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The game has text-based graphics and is open-ended with no main objectives. It is part <a href="/wiki/Construction_and_management_simulation" title="Construction and management simulation">construction and management simulation</a> and part <a href="/wiki/Roguelike" title="Roguelike">roguelike</a> game due to its multiple game modes. Before playing, the player has to generate worlds with continents, oceans and histories documenting civilizations. The main game mode, Dwarf Fortress mode, consists of selecting a suitable site from the generated-world, establishing a successful colony or fortress, combating threats like goblin invasions, generating wealth, and taking care of the dwarves. Each dwarf is modeled down to its individual personality, has likes or dislikes, and specific trainable skills in various labors.<sup id="cite_ref-IGNSpencer2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNSpencer2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The second game mode, Adventurer mode, is a turn-based, open-ended roguelike, where the player starts off as either an adventurer in the world, or the leader of an adventuring group, and is free to explore, complete quests, or even visit old abandoned fortresses.<sup id="cite_ref-GIPearson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GIPearson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The combat system is anatomically detailed with combat logs describing organs getting pierced, fat getting bruised, and limbs getting severed.<sup id="cite_ref-PCGHogartya_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PCGHogartya-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RPSSmith1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPSSmith1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Continuing its development, Tarn calls it his life's work and said in 2011 that version 1.0 will not be ready for at least another 20 years, and even after that he would continue to work on it. &#160;The game influenced <i><a href="/wiki/Minecraft" title="Minecraft">Minecraft</a></i> and was selected among other games to be featured in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> to show the history of video gaming in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There is an active community of fans of the game, and Tarn said they have helped him in game development in addition to giving monetary support.<sup id="cite_ref-GRose_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GRose-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Fans have also made creative interpretations of the game. He and his brother send crayon drawings or short stories to the donors, customized to their requests and display the donors who have donated the most on their website.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inspiration_and_preferences">Inspiration and preferences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tarn_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inspiration and preferences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tarn has cited books, movies, <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">pen and paper role-playing games</a> and other computer games, experienced during his childhood, as an inspiration for the various games he developed.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He said that <a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a>, works of <a href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" class="mw-redirect" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a> and tabletop games like <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberpunk_2020" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyberpunk 2020">Cyberpunk 2020</a></i> interested him,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">sci-fi</a> being heavily influential. On <i>Dwarf Fortress'</i> wound-based system rather than regular <a href="/wiki/Hitpoints" class="mw-redirect" title="Hitpoints">hitpoints</a>, he said, "Hit points are depressing to me. It's sort of a reflex to just have HP/MP, like a game designer stopped doing their job...You should really question all of the mechanics in the game from the bottom up."<sup id="cite_ref-Gamasutra_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gamasutra-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He said he does not use <a href="/wiki/Version_control" title="Version control">version control</a> for his works.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Regarding his career, he said, "...but as far as design is concerned, I just think that I've happened to fall into a little sweet spot where I get a lot of freedom, but I guess the cost is my livelihood."<sup id="cite_ref-Escapist_article_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escapist_article-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He further said working in the gaming industry is "soul crushing".<sup id="cite_ref-Stanford_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adams has shown his dislike for <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Massively multiplayer online games">massively multiplayer online games</a> and said that popular games are addictive because they make use of the player's <a href="/wiki/Compulsive_hoarding" title="Compulsive hoarding">compulsive hoarding</a> trait.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
