<p><b>Danielle Bunten Berry</b> (February 19, 1949 – July 3, 1998),<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  also known as <b>Dan Bunten</b>, was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Game_designer" class="mw-redirect" title="Game designer">game designer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Game programmer">programmer</a>, known for the 1983 game <i><a href="/wiki/M.U.L.E." title="M.U.L.E.">M.U.L.E.</a></i>, one of the first influential multiplayer games, and 1984's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Cities_of_Gold_(video_game)" title="The Seven Cities of Gold (video game)">The Seven Cities of Gold</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1998, she was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Lifetime_Achievement_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Lifetime Achievement Award">Lifetime Achievement Award</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">Computer Game Developers Association</a>. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Interactive_Arts_%26_Sciences" title="Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences">Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences</a> chose Bunten as the 10th inductee into its <a href="/wiki/AIAS_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="AIAS Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_of_Fame_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_of_Fame-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bunten was born in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis, Missouri</a> and moved to <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a> as a junior in high school.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She was the oldest of six siblings. While growing up in Little Rock, Bunten's family didn't always have enough money to make ends meet, so Bunten took a job at a pharmacy. She also held a leadership role with the Boy Scouts.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Bunten, one of her fondest childhood memories involved playing games with her family. She was quoted saying, “When I was a kid, the only times my family spent together that weren’t totally dysfunctional were when we were playing games. Consequently, I believe games are a wonderful way to socialize.”<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While attending the University of Arkansas, she opened up her own bike shop called Highroller Cyclerie.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Bunten acquired a degree in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_engineering" title="Industrial engineering">industrial engineering</a> in 1974 and started programming text-based <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> as a hobby. After she graduated from college, she was employed by the National Science Foundation, where she created urban models before starting a job at a video game company.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1978, Bunten sold a real-time auction game for the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> titled <i>Wheeler Dealers</i> to a <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> software company, Speakeasy Software. This early multiplayer game required a custom controller, raising its price to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>$35 in an era of $15 games sold in plastic bags. It sold only 50 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-Halcyon_Days_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halcyon_Days-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After producing three titles for <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Simulations_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Strategic Simulations Inc.">SSI</a>, Bunten, who by then had founded a software company called Ozark Softscape, caught the attention of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Trip_Hawkins" title="Trip Hawkins">Trip Hawkins</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/M.U.L.E." title="M.U.L.E.">M.U.L.E.</a></i> was Bunten's first game for EA, originally published for the <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">Atari 8-bit family</a> because the Atari 800 had four controller ports. Bunten later ported it to the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a>. While its sales - 30,000 units - were not high, the game developed a cult following and was widely <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement_of_software" class="mw-redirect" title="Copyright infringement of software">pirated</a>. The game setting was inspired by the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Enough_for_Love" title="Time Enough for Love">Time Enough for Love</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Halcyon_Days_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halcyon_Days-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Along with the success of M.U.L.E., Berry also had close ties with the games <i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Rascals" title="Robot Rascals">Robot Rascals</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Africa" title="Heart of Africa">Heart of Africa</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Cartels_%26_Cutthroat$" class="mw-redirect" title="Cartels &amp; Cutthroat$">Cartels &amp; Cutthroat$</a></i>.  Throughout her career, she was involved in the creation of 12 games, 10 of which revolved around multiplayer compatibility. The only two which didn't have a multiplayer focus were <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Cities_of_Gold" title="Seven Cities of Gold">Seven Cities of Gold</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Africa" title="Heart of Africa">Heart of Africa</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bunten wanted to follow up <i><a href="/wiki/M.U.L.E." title="M.U.L.E.">M.U.L.E.</a></i> with a game that would have been similar to the later game <i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_video_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Civilization video game">Civilization</a></i>, but after fellow Ozark Softscape partners balked at the idea, Bunten followed with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Cities_of_Gold_(game)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Seven Cities of Gold (game)">The Seven Cities of Gold</a></i>, which proved popular because of its simplicity. By the time the continent data were stored in the computer's memory, there was little left for fancy graphics or complex gameplay - the game had only five resources. It was a hit, selling more than 150,000 copies.
</p><p>The follow-up game, <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Africa" title="Heart of Africa">Heart of Africa</a></i>, appeared in 1985 and was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Robot_Rascals" title="Robot Rascals">Robot Rascals</a></i>, a combination computer/card game that had no single-player mode and sold only 9,000 copies, and 1988's <i><a href="/wiki/Modem_Wars" title="Modem Wars">Modem Wars</a></i>, one of the early games played by two players over a dial-up <a href="/wiki/Modem" title="Modem">modem</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Modem Wars was ahead of its time, as few people in the late 1980s had modems in their homes.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bunten departed EA for <a href="/wiki/MicroProse" title="MicroProse">MicroProse</a>. Allegedly, <a href="/wiki/Trip_Hawkins" title="Trip Hawkins">Trip Hawkins</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">EA</a>, did not feel that pushing production of games onto a cartridge based system was a good idea. The shift was important to Bunten, as computer games had previously been distributed on floppy discs, and a changeover to a cartridge system would allow games to be played on Nintendo systems.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This was a significant factor in her decision to leave. She then developed a computer version of the board game <i><a href="/wiki/Axis_%26_Allies" title="Axis &amp; Allies">Axis and Allies</a></i>, which became 1990's <i><a href="/wiki/Command_HQ" title="Command HQ">Command HQ</a></i>, a modem/network <a href="/wiki/Grand_strategy_wargame" title="Grand strategy wargame">grand strategy wargame</a>. Bunten's second and last game for MicroProse was 1992's <i><a href="/wiki/Global_Conquest" title="Global Conquest">Global Conquest</a></i>, a 4-player network/modem war game. It was the first 4-player network game from a major publisher. Bunten was a strong advocate of <a href="/wiki/Multiplayer_video_game" title="Multiplayer video game">multi-player online games</a>, observing that, "No one ever said on their deathbed, 'Gee, I wish I had spent more time alone with my computer.'"<sup id="cite_ref-anticlock-why_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anticlock-why-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A port of <i>M.U.L.E.</i> to the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Mega Drive/Genesis</a> was cancelled after Bunten refused to put guns and bombs in the game, feeling it would alter the game too much from its original concept.<sup id="cite_ref-gama_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gama-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  In 1997, Bunten shifted focus to multiplayer games over the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> with <i>Warsport</i>, a remake of <i><a href="/wiki/Modem_Wars" title="Modem Wars">Modem Wars</a></i> that debuted on the <a href="/wiki/MPlayer.com" title="MPlayer.com">MPlayer.com</a> game network.
</p><p>Less than a year after the release of <i>Warsport</i>, Bunten was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> presumably related to years of heavy smoking.<sup id="cite_ref-salon_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> She died on July 3, 1998. At the time, she was working on the design of an Internet version of <i><a href="/wiki/M.U.L.E." title="M.U.L.E.">M.U.L.E.</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Halcyon_Days_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halcyon_Days-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="M.U.L.E"><i>M.U.L.E</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: M.U.L.E">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/M.U.L.E." title="M.U.L.E.">M.U.L.E.</a></div>
<p>The game's primary premise consisted of players playing with and against one another to establish total control over a planet.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The name of the game stands for Multiple Use Labor Element.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The game was originally made for the <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">Atari 400</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">Atari 800</a> but was later developed to be compatible with the <a href="/wiki/Commodore_64" title="Commodore 64">Commodore 64</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">NES</a>, and <a href="/wiki/IBM_PCjr" title="IBM PCjr">IBM pcjr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The game has a maximum of four players. Players are given different options and choices, and are allowed to create their colony the way they see fit. This can be done by changing races and giving respective colonies different advantages that will impact the way the game is played and determined later on down the line.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Ultimately there are two ways in which players can win the game. The first way is by having the most amount of money out of all four players, and the second way is by being able to survive the colony itself.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The game focuses heavily on going out and retrieving resources that can be used to benefit their character.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Items such as food, energy, and crystite are some of a number of in-game items that players are able to retrieve and use to better themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In order for a player to be able to access these items, they will first have to have access to a M.U.L.E.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The acquisition of these items has a direct reflection on what the player will be allowed to do. For example, if a player doesn't have enough food, they will have less time during their turn.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Seven_Cities_of_Gold"><i>The Seven Cities of Gold</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Seven Cities of Gold">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Seven_Cities_of_Gold_(video_game)" title="The Seven Cities of Gold (video game)">The Seven Cities of Gold (video game)</a></div>
<p><i>The Seven Cities of Gold</i> was originally intended to be another multiplayer game.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It was originally a single player format, focused heavily on having the players travel around the map and collect items to help them strengthen their colony. Once they felt as though they had a solid colony, the players could battle each other to see who could overtake who.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> After much consideration, Ozark Software came to the conclusion that this would not be doable. Instead, they went with a formula that had the game focus solely on developing a colony.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozark_Softscape">Ozark Softscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ozark Softscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ozark Softscape was a computer game development team consisting initially of Danielle Bunten, her brother Bill Bunten, Jim Rushing, and Alan Watson. Ozark was run out of Bunten's basement.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The company was based out of <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a> and had profound success with a few of their early titles. Ozark Softscape had a publishing deal with Electronic Arts for several of its groundbreaking games. In the early 1990s, Ozark Softscape left its partnership with Electronic Arts over a dispute to port some games to cartridge format for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_Entertainment_System" title="Nintendo Entertainment System">Nintendo Entertainment System</a>. It began a partnership with MicroProse to produce two more titles: <i><a href="/wiki/Command_HQ" title="Command HQ">Command HQ</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Global_Conquest" title="Global Conquest">Global Conquest</a></i>. A dispute occurred over creating a follow-up to <i>M.U.L.E.</i> with <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a> in 1993, and the company dissolved. The employees of Ozark Softscape moved to different areas of the software industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bunten was married three times. Bunten had three children, one daughter and two sons.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a third divorce, Bunten, who had until then been living as male, transitioned to living as a woman. Bunten underwent <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">sex reassignment surgery</a> in November 1992 and afterward kept a lower profile in the games industry. Bunten later regretted having surgery, finding that for her, the drawbacks of surgical transition outweighed the benefits, and wishing she had considered alternative approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-anticlock_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anticlock-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> She joked that the surgery was to improve the <a href="/wiki/Women_and_video_games" title="Women and video games">video game industry's male/female ratio</a> and aesthetics,<sup id="cite_ref-cgw199308_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cgw199308-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but advised others considering a sex change not to proceed unless there was no alternative and warned them of the cost, saying "Being my 'real self' could have included having a penis and including more femininity in whatever forms made sense. I didn't know that until too late and now I have to make the best of the life I've stumbled into. I just wish I would have tried more options before I jumped off the precipice."<sup id="cite_ref-anticlock_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anticlock-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>After her transition in fall 1992, Bunten stayed out of the video game spotlight, mostly keeping to herself. She felt as though that after transitioning she was not as good at video game development as she had previously been, stating "So, I'm a little more than three years into my new life role as Ms. Danielle Berry, and her career looks to be somewhat different from old Mr. Dan Bunten's. For one thing, I'm not as good a programmer as he was."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 3, 1998, Berry died of lung cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Bunten_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although many of Bunten's titles were not commercially successful, they were widely recognized by the industry as being ahead of their time. On May 7, 1998, less than two months before her death, Berry was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Lifetime_Achievement_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Lifetime Achievement Award">Lifetime Achievement Award</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Game_Developers_Association" title="International Game Developers Association">Computer Game Developers Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Will_Wright_(game_designer)" title="Will Wright (game designer)">Will Wright</a> dedicated his blockbuster hit <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sims" title="The Sims">The Sims</a></i> to Bunten's memory. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Interactive_Arts_%26_Sciences" title="Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences">Academy of Interactive Arts &amp; Sciences</a> chose Bunten to be inducted into its <a href="/wiki/AIAS_Hall_of_Fame" class="mw-redirect" title="AIAS Hall of Fame">Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall_of_Fame_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_of_Fame-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Sid Meier, the mastermind behind the video game series <i>Civilization</i>, inducted her at the Hard Rock Hotel in Las Vegas.
</p><p>Bunten was a true pioneer for the video game industry, especially its multiplayer aspect of it. She is regarded by many as one of the best designers to ever grace the video game industry. Her success has even led people to make the claim that the work she did with games like M.U.L.E and Seven Cities of Gold was the inspiration behind highly successful modern multiplayer games like World of Warcraft.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bunten was known as someone who was very easy to talk to. If someone recognized her in public, she would be more than delighted to have a conversation with them.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
