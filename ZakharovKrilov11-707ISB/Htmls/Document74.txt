<p><b>Scott Elliott Fahlman</b> (born March 21, 1948) is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a> <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">Emeritus</a> at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Language_Technologies_Institute" title="Language Technologies Institute">Language Technologies Institute</a> and Computer Science Department.  He is notable for early work on <a href="/wiki/Automated_planning_and_scheduling" title="Automated planning and scheduling">automated planning and scheduling</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Blocks_world" title="Blocks world">blocks world</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Semantic_network" title="Semantic network">semantic networks</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Neural_network" title="Neural network">neural networks</a> (especially the cascade correlation algorithm), on the <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> <a href="/wiki/Dylan_(programming_language)" title="Dylan (programming language)">Dylan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Common_Lisp" title="Common Lisp">Common Lisp</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/CMU_Common_Lisp" title="CMU Common Lisp">CMU Common Lisp</a>), and he was one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Lucid_Inc." title="Lucid Inc.">Lucid Inc.</a>. During the period when it was <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standardized</a>, he was recognized as "the leader of Common Lisp."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> From 2006 to 2015, Fahlman was engaged in developing a <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_base" title="Knowledge base">knowledge base</a> named <i>Scone</i>, based in part on his thesis work on the NETL Semantic Network.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Fahlman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fahlman was born in <a href="/wiki/Medina,_Ohio" title="Medina, Ohio">Medina, Ohio</a>, the son of Lorna May (Dean) and John Emil Fahlman. He attended the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT), where he received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (B.S.) and <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> (M.S.) degree in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> in 1973, and a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> (Ph.D.) in <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> in 1977. His master's thesis advisor was <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Winston" title="Patrick Winston">Patrick Winston</a> and his doctoral thesis advisor was <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Sussman" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerald Sussman">Gerald Sussman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He has noted that his doctoral diploma says the degree was awarded for "original research as demonstrated by a thesis in the field of Artificial Intelligence" and suggested that it may be the first doctorate to use that term.<sup id="cite_ref-Quora_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Quora-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He is a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">fellow</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_Artificial_Intelligence" class="mw-redirect" title="American Association for Artificial Intelligence">American Association for Artificial Intelligence</a>.
</p><p>Fahlman acted as thesis advisor for Donald Cohen, David B. McDonald, <a href="/wiki/David_S._Touretzky" title="David S. Touretzky">David S. Touretzky</a>, Skef Wholey, Justin Boyan, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Witbrock" title="Michael Witbrock">Michael Witbrock</a>, and Alicia Tribble Sagae.
</p><p>From May 1996 to July 2001, Fahlman directed the <a href="/wiki/Justsystem_Pittsburgh_Research_Center" title="Justsystem Pittsburgh Research Center">Justsystem Pittsburgh Research Center</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emoticons">Emoticons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scott_Fahlman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Emoticons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fahlman was not the first to <i>suggest</i> the concept of the emoticon – a similar concept for a marker appeared in an article of <i><a href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest" title="Reader&#39;s Digest">Reader's Digest</a></i> in May 1967, although that idea was never put into practice.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
In an interview printed in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> in 1969, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a> noted: </p><blockquote><p>"I often think there should exist a special typographical sign for a smile –
 some sort of concave mark, a supine round bracket."<sup id="cite_ref-Nabokov1973_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nabokov1973-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Fahlman is credited with originating the first <a href="/wiki/Smiley" title="Smiley">smiley</a> <a href="/wiki/Emoticon" title="Emoticon">emoticon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which he thought would help people on a message board at Carnegie Mellon to distinguish serious posts from jokes. He proposed the use of <code>:-)</code> and <code>:-(</code> for this purpose, and the symbols caught on. The original message from which these symbols originated was posted on 19 September 1982. The message was recovered by Jeff Baird on 10 September 2002 and read:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<pre style="border: 0px;">19-Sep-82 11:44    Scott E  Fahlman             :-)
From: Scott E  Fahlman &lt;Fahlman at Cmu-20c&gt;

I propose that the following character sequence for joke markers:

:-)

Read it sideways.  Actually, it is probably more economical to mark
things that are NOT jokes, given current trends.  For this, use

:-(
</pre>
