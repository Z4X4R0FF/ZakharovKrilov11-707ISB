<p><b>John Warner Backus</b> (December 3, 1924 – March 17, 2007) was an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>. He directed the team that invented and implemented <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">FORTRAN</a>, the first widely used  <a href="/wiki/High-level_programming_language" title="High-level programming language">high-level programming language</a>, and was the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Backus–Naur form">Backus–Naur form</a> (BNF), a widely used notation to define <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">formal language</a> <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>. He later did research into the <a href="/wiki/Function-level_programming" title="Function-level programming">function-level programming</a> paradigm, presenting his findings in his influential 1977 Turing Award lecture "Can Programming Be Liberated from the von Neumann Style?<sup id="cite_ref-can_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-can-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">IEEE</a> awarded Backus the <a href="/wiki/W._Wallace_McDowell_Award" title="W. Wallace McDowell Award">W. W. McDowell Award</a> in 1967 for the development of FORTRAN.<sup id="cite_ref-McDowell_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDowell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He received the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> in 1975<sup id="cite_ref-National_Science_Foundation_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Science_Foundation-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the 1977 <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">ACM Turing Award</a>" for profound, influential, and lasting contributions to the design of practical high-level programming systems, notably through his work on FORTRAN, and for publication of formal procedures for the specification of programming languages".<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He retired in 1991 and died at his home in <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Oregon" title="Ashland, Oregon">Ashland, Oregon</a> on March 17, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-nytobit_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Backus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Backus was born in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and grew up in nearby <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington, Delaware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He studied at <a href="/wiki/The_Hill_School" title="The Hill School">The Hill School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pottstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Pottstown, Pennsylvania">Pottstown, Pennsylvania</a>, and was apparently not a diligent student.<sup id="cite_ref-nytobit_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He entered college at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> to study <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, but struggled with his classes there, and was expelled after less than a year for poor attendance.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He was subsequently conscripted into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nytobit_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and eventually came to hold the rank of corporal, being put in command of an anti-aircraft battery stationed at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Stewart" title="Fort Stewart">Fort Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>After receiving high scores on a military aptitude test, the Army sent him to study engineering at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pittsburgh" title="University of Pittsburgh">University of Pittsburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He later transferred to a pre-medical program at <a href="/wiki/Haverford_College" title="Haverford College">Haverford College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During an internship at a hospital, he was diagnosed with a cranial <a href="/wiki/Bone_tumor" title="Bone tumor">bone tumor</a>, which was successfully removed, and a plate was installed in his head. He then moved to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Medical_College" title="New York Medical College">Flower and Fifth Avenue Medical School</a> for medical school, but found it uninteresting and dropped out after nine months.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He soon underwent a second operation to replace the metal plate in his head with one of his own design,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and received an honorable medical discharge from the U.S. Army in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortran">Fortran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Backus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fortran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Backus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2017</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>After moving to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> he trained initially as a <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> technician and became interested in mathematics. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> with a bachelor's degree in 1949 and a master's degree in 1950, both in mathematics,<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and joined <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> in 1950. During his first three years, he worked on the <a href="/wiki/IBM_SSEC" title="IBM SSEC">Selective Sequence Electronic Calculator (SSEC)</a>; his first major project was to write a program to calculate positions of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>. In 1953 Backus developed the language <a href="/wiki/Speedcoding" title="Speedcoding">Speedcoding</a>, the first high-level language created for an IBM computer, to aid in software development for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_701" title="IBM 701">IBM 701</a> computer.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Programming was very difficult at this time, and in 1954 Backus assembled a team to define and develop <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_704" title="IBM 704">IBM 704</a> computer. <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> was the first high-level programming language to be put to broad use.
</p>
<h2><span id="Backus.E2.80.93Naur_form"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Backus–Naur_form">Backus–Naur form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Backus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Backus–Naur form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Backus–Naur form">Backus–Naur form</a></div>
<p>Backus served on the international committees that developed <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_58" title="ALGOL 58">ALGOL 58</a> and the very influential <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_60" title="ALGOL 60">ALGOL 60</a>, which quickly became the <i>de facto</i> worldwide standard for publishing <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a>. Backus developed the <a href="/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form" title="Backus–Naur form">Backus–Naur form</a> (BNF), published in the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> report on ALGOL 58. It was a formal notation able to describe any <a href="/wiki/Context-free" class="mw-redirect" title="Context-free">context-free</a> programming language, and was important in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_compiler_construction" title="History of compiler construction">development of compilers</a>. A few deviations from this approach were tried—notably in <a href="/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language)" title="Lisp (programming language)">Lisp</a> and <a href="/wiki/APL_(programming_language)" title="APL (programming language)">APL</a>—but by the 1970s, following the development of automated compiler generators such as <a href="/wiki/Yacc" title="Yacc">yacc</a>, Backus–Naur context-free specifications for computer languages had become quite standard. This contribution helped Backus win the <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> in 1977.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function-level_programming">Function-level programming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Backus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Function-level programming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Backus later worked on a <a href="/wiki/Function-level_programming" title="Function-level programming">function-level programming language</a> known as <a href="/wiki/FP_(programming_language)" title="FP (programming language)">FP</a>, which was described in his <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> lecture "Can Programming be Liberated from the <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_programming_languages" title="Von Neumann programming languages">von Neumann Style</a>?".<sup id="cite_ref-can_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-can-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes viewed as Backus's apology for creating Fortran, this paper did less to garner interest in the FP language than to spark research into <a href="/wiki/Functional_programming" title="Functional programming">functional programming</a> in general. When Backus publicized the function-level style of programming, his message was mostly misunderstood<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> as being the same as traditional functional programming style languages.
</p><p>FP was strongly inspired by <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_E._Iverson" title="Kenneth E. Iverson">Kenneth E. Iverson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/APL_programming_language" class="mw-redirect" title="APL programming language">APL</a>, even using a non-standard <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">character set</a>. An FP <a href="/wiki/Interpreter_(computing)" title="Interpreter (computing)">interpreter</a> was distributed with the <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Software_Distribution" title="Berkeley Software Distribution">4.2BSD</a> <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> operating system, but there were relatively few implementations of the language, most of which were used for educational purposes.
</p><p>Backus spent the latter part of his career developing <a href="/wiki/FL_(programming_language)" title="FL (programming language)">FL</a> (from "Function Level"), a successor to FP. FL was an internal IBM research project, and development of the language stopped when the project was finished. Only a few papers documenting it remain, and the source code of the compiler described in them was not made public. FL was at odds with functional programming languages being developed in the 1980s, most of which were based on the <a href="/wiki/Lambda_calculus" title="Lambda calculus">lambda calculus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Static_typing" class="mw-redirect" title="Static typing">static typing</a> systems instead of, as in APL, the concatenation of primitive operations. Many of the language's ideas have now been implemented in versions of the <a href="/wiki/J_(programming_language)" title="J (programming language)">J programming language</a>, Iverson's successor to APL.
</p>
