	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >Les Earnest</h1>
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		<div id="contentSub2"></div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Lester Donald Earnest</b> (born December 17, 1930) is a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>. After receiving his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">B.S.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> from the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> (CalTech) in 1953, he began his career as a <a href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a> in 1954 during a stint as a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> Aviation Electronics Officer &amp; Digital Computer Project Officer at the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Development_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval Air Development Center">Naval Air Development Center</a> (NADC) in <a href="/wiki/Johnsville,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Johnsville, Pennsylvania">Johnsville, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-biography_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biography-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1956, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT)'s <a href="/wiki/MIT_Lincoln_Laboratory" title="MIT Lincoln Laboratory">Lincoln Laboratory</a> to help design the <a href="/wiki/Semi-Automatic_Ground_Environment" title="Semi-Automatic Ground Environment">Semi-Automatic Ground Environment</a> (SAGE) air defense system.<sup id="cite_ref-biography_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biography-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, the focus of his career shifted to innovations within the field of word processing. During this time, he was responsible for developing the "first pen-based computer system that reliably recognized cursive writing"<sup id="cite_ref-patents_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patents-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the first spell checker.<sup id="cite_ref-biography_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biography-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> From 1959 to 1965, he was a subdepartment head at the <a href="/wiki/MITRE_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="MITRE Corporation">MITRE Corporation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bedford,_Massachusetts" title="Bedford, Massachusetts">Bedford, Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>. While at MITRE, he received an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">M.S.</a> in electrical engineering from MIT in 1960.
</p><p>In 1965, Earnest became a lecturer in computer science at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> and the chief administrative officer of the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory</a> (SAIL). Under founding director <a href="/wiki/John_McCarthy_(computer_scientist)" title="John McCarthy (computer scientist)">John McCarthy</a>, he became involved with the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network (<a href="/wiki/ARPAnet" class="mw-redirect" title="ARPAnet">ARPAnet</a>) startup committee. This association would lead him to the one innovation he has received the most acclaim for: the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Finger_protocol" title="Finger protocol">Finger protocol</a>  (<style In the late 1960s, Earnest continued to diversify the types of technologies he involved himself with. He made significant contributions in the fields of robotics through the creation of systems that coupled <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">computer vision</a> with prosthetic and vehicular applications.<sup id="cite_ref-biography_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biography-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following SAIL's merger with the University's computer science department in 1980, he left Stanford to serve as the founding president and director of Imagen Corp. in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>. He returned to Stanford's computer science department in 1985 as a lecturer and associate department chair before retiring as senior research computer scientist emeritus in 1988.
</p><p>Accomplishments in computing aside, Earnest is also a long-time bicycle enthusiast. He has served as director and/or officer in several prominent cycling associations including the <a href="/wiki/USA_Cycling" title="USA Cycling">U.S. Cycling Federation</a>, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060705211652/http://www.usbhof.com/home/">U.S. Bicycling Hall of Fame</a>, and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060718234357/http://www.fiac.us/">Federation of Independent Associations for Cycling</a>.
</p>
