<p><b>Steven</b><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> "<b>Steve Tiberius</b><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>" <b>Gibson</b> (born 26 March 1955) is an American <a href="/wiki/Software_engineer" title="Software engineer">software engineer</a>, security researcher, and IT security proponent. In the early 1980s, Gibson worked on light pen technology for use with Apple and Atari systems. In 1985, he founded Gibson Research Corporation,  best known for its <a href="/wiki/SpinRite" title="SpinRite">SpinRite</a> software.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Gibson_(computer_programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibson started working on computers as a teenager, and got his first computing job with <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> lab when he was 15 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-MillarGuardian_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillarGuardian-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Gibson writes he studied electrical engineering and computer science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> while the alumni directory shows he did not.<sup id="cite_ref-GibsonResume_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GibsonResume-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Gibson_(computer_programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gibson was hired as a programmer for <a href="/wiki/California_Pacific_Computer_Company" title="California Pacific Computer Company">California Pacific Computer Company</a> in 1980, where he worked on copy protection for the company's products.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gibson founded Gibson Laboratories in <a href="/wiki/Laguna_Hills,_California" title="Laguna Hills, California">Laguna Hills, California</a> in 1981; Gibson Labs developed a <a href="/wiki/Light_pen" title="Light pen">light pen</a> for the Apple II, Atari, and other platforms and went out of business in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-GibsonResume_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GibsonResume-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1985 Gibson founded Gibson Research Corporation (GRC) - a computer <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> firm.<sup id="cite_ref-GibsonResume_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GibsonResume-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1986 to 1993 Gibson wrote the "Tech Talk" column for <i><a href="/wiki/InfoWorld" title="InfoWorld">InfoWorld</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, Gibson created one of the first <a href="/wiki/Adware" title="Adware">adware</a> removal programs, which he called OptOut.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, Gibson predicted that Microsoft's implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Raw_socket" class="mw-redirect" title="Raw socket">SOCK_RAW</a> protocol in the initial release of <a href="/wiki/Windows_XP" title="Windows XP">Windows XP</a> would lead to widespread chaos by making it easier for Windows XP users to create <a href="/wiki/Denial_of_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Denial of service">denial of service</a> (DoS) attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In that year, his company's website was brought down by a DoS attack;<sup id="cite_ref-MillarGuardian_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillarGuardian-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the attacks continued for two weeks. Gibson blogged about the attacks and his (ultimately successful) efforts to track down the hacker.<sup id="cite_ref-MillarGuardian_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillarGuardian-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Three years after the Windows XP release, Microsoft limited raw socket support in <a href="/wiki/Windows_XP#Service_Pack_2" title="Windows XP">Service Pack 2</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005 Gibson launched a weekly <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcast</a> called "<a href="/wiki/Security_Now" title="Security Now">Security Now</a>" with <a href="/wiki/Leo_Laporte" title="Leo Laporte">Leo Laporte</a> on <a href="/wiki/TWiT.tv" title="TWiT.tv">TWiT.tv</a>, with its archives hosted on GRC's website.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006 Gibson raised the possibility that the <a href="/wiki/Windows_Metafile_vulnerability" title="Windows Metafile vulnerability">Windows Metafile vulnerability</a> bug was actually a <a href="/wiki/Backdoor_(computing)" title="Backdoor (computing)">backdoor</a> intentionally engineered into the system.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  A response by Microsoft<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Russinovich" title="Mark Russinovich">Mark Russinovich</a> on Microsoft's Technet<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> stated that the bug appeared to be coding error and that Gibson's reasoning was based upon Microsoft's abort procedure documentation being misleading. Furthermore, Peter Ferrie of Symantec Security Response, USA noted that:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Gibson claimed that a thread is created to run the SetAbortProc handler. In fact, no thread is created to run the handler – it is a callback, which is called by the parser, and the parser has to wait until the callback returns, otherwise the whole point of the function (to abort the printing) is lost. By his own admission, Gibson did not read the documentation (in fact, he claimed that he couldn’t find it, although it is freely available on Microsoft’s Web site), and he claimed that the device context is not available to the function handler. Of course the device context is available to the function handler &#8212; it is one of the two parameters that is passed to it (see above), and it is required in order to abort the printing. Finally, Gibson claimed that the control flow could not return to Windows. It is simply a matter of the function returning and discarding the parameters that were passed on the stack. If the record is well formed, Windows will continue to parse the file, as before.
</p><p>.<br />
.<br />
.<br />
</p><p>
Gibson admits that he was guessing about a number of things. Unfortunately, he guessed poorly. I guess we know better now.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In 2013 Gibson proposed <a href="/wiki/SQRL" title="SQRL">SQRL</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> as a way to simplify the process of authentication without the risk of revelation of information about the transaction to a third party.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GRC_products">GRC products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Gibson_(computer_programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: GRC products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GRC has created a number of <a href="/wiki/Utility_software" title="Utility software">utilities</a>, most of which are <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>DNS Benchmark, freeware that lets users test the performance of the domain name servers used by their internet service providers.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Securable, freeware to test whether a pre-<a href="/wiki/Windows_7" title="Windows 7">Windows 7</a> computer is 64-bit compatible. It also tells the user if <a href="/wiki/Data_Execution_Prevention" class="mw-redirect" title="Data Execution Prevention">Data Execution Prevention</a> is enabled.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shields_Up" class="mw-redirect" title="Shields Up">Shields Up</a>, a free browser-based firewall testing service; one of the oldest available<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SpinRite" title="SpinRite">SpinRite</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard disk">hard disk</a> scanning and <a href="/wiki/Data_recovery" title="Data recovery">data recovery</a> utility first released in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-pcmag1_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag1-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>   As of January 2019 the current version was 6.0,<sup id="cite_ref-SpinRite_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpinRite-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> which was first released in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  SpinRite is a commercial product, costing $89 as of January 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-SpinRite_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpinRite-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  Gibson's work on SpinRite has led to him being considered an expert on hard drive failure.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Spoofarino, freeware released in 2006 and promised since the controversy over the launch of Windows XP in 2001, it enables users to test whether their internet service providers allow them to send forged or "spoofed" packets of data to Gibson's Web site.<sup id="cite_ref-SpoofarinoLaunch_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpoofarinoLaunch-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Never10, standalone freeware program that toggles registry values in Windows 7, 8, and 8.1, which either disables or enables Microsoft's Get Windows 10 app and automatic OS upgrade. As of version 1.3 also triggers the removal of any previously downloaded <a href="/wiki/Windows_10" title="Windows 10">Windows 10</a> upgrade files as part of the disable function.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>InSpectre, a utility that examines a computer's vulnerability to the <a href="/wiki/Meltdown_(security_vulnerability)" title="Meltdown (security vulnerability)">Meltdown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spectre_(security_vulnerability)" title="Spectre (security vulnerability)">Spectre</a> attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
