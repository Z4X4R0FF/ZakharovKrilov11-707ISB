<p><b>Susan Bond</b> (born 1942), was a scientific officer and computer <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a> for the Mathematics Division of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Radar_Establishment" title="Royal Radar Establishment">Royal Radar Establishment</a> (RRE) in the United Kingdom. She worked extensively on the <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Radar_Establishment_Automatic_Computer" title="Royal Radar Establishment Automatic Computer">Royal Radar Establishment Automatic Computer</a> (RREAC), an early <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_electronics" title="Solid-state electronics">solid-state electronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/ICT_1900_series" title="ICT 1900 series">ICL 1907F</a> computer.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IFIP_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_G._Bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bond was born in 1942 and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Dagenham" title="Dagenham">Dagenham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (UK). Both her parents were teachers, and she was an only child.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> She studied at <a href="/wiki/Bristol_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Bristol University">Bristol University</a> from 1962 to 1965, where she studied mathematics and science and received <a href="/wiki/First-class_honours" class="mw-redirect" title="First-class honours">first-class honours</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_research">Career and research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_G._Bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career and research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After graduating from Bristol, Bond was interested in working in applied mathematics, although she didn't have computer training before then. She applied to and joined the Mathematics Division of the RRE in 1965;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> she was hired by British <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> and engineer <a href="/wiki/Philip_Woodward" title="Philip Woodward">Philip Woodward</a>. Her work mostly consisted of writing <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a>, not "numerical" computing.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At the start of her career, Bond was the only female scientific officer with a graduate education at RRE.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Bond later learned that her supervisor Woodward had been, as historian Janet Abbate describes, "'actively recruiting women' as an affordable source of high-quality researchers".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of her first projects was reimplementing <i>Syntax Improving Device</i> (SID), a <a href="/wiki/Compiler-compiler" title="Compiler-compiler">compiler-compiler</a> tool developed by fellow RRE employee Michael Foster, to generate compilers for <a href="/wiki/High-level_programming_language" title="High-level programming language">high-level programming languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Afterward, she worked with Ian Currie on <a href="/wiki/CORAL" title="CORAL">CORAL</a> 64, a high-level language for <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">embedded system</a> computers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementing_ALGOL_68">Implementing ALGOL 68</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_G._Bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Implementing ALGOL 68">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For technical descriptions, see <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68-R" title="ALGOL 68-R">ALGOL 68-R</a> and <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68RS" title="ALGOL 68RS">ALGOL 68RS</a>.</div>
<p>The RRE had originally used <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_60" title="ALGOL 60">ALGOL 60</a> for the RREAC from its initial development in 1963. After the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Information_Processing" title="International Federation for Information Processing">International Federation for Information Processing</a> (IFIP) published the specifications for the more powerful <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a> in 1968, RRE attempted to adapt it for use on the RREAC. Bond worked with John Morison and Ian Currie on ALGOL 68-R, the first compiler implementation of ALGOL 68, and they announced its creation at the 20–24 July 1970 <i>IFIP Working Conference on ALGOL 68 Implementation</i> in Munich.<sup id="cite_ref-Malvern_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malvern-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IFIP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Their ALGOL 68-R was an adaptation of the ALGOL 60 compiler they had built for RREAC. The team that worked on ALGOL 68-R intended for the language to become the RRE's primary programming language, which could be used for scientific programming as well as business administration tasks like payroll and taking inventory.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IFIP_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the publication of the ALGOL 68-R specifications, Bond and Woodward published a narrative guide to ALGOL 68, titled "ALGOL 68-R User’s Guide" through <a href="/wiki/HM_Stationery_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="HM Stationery Office">HM Stationery Office</a>. The initial 17,000 copy run sold out.<sup id="cite_ref-Malvern_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malvern-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Bond effectively provided ongoing support for the compiler: readers would contact her whenever they had trouble implementing it. Bond and Woodward continued to update and publish new versions of their guide for the RRE's later implementations of ALGOL, such as <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68RS" title="ALGOL 68RS">ALGOL 68RS</a>. One reviewer, Richard Shreeve, contested that while their 1983 title <i>Guide to ALGOL 68 for Users of RS Systems</i> was an "excellent reference text", it gives "insufficient help to the beginner or newcomer to ALGOL 68".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Promotion_to_superintendent">Promotion to superintendent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_G._Bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Promotion to superintendent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1976, the RRE merged with several other research institutions into a renamed <a href="/wiki/Royal_Signals_and_Radar_Establishment" title="Royal Signals and Radar Establishment">Royal Signals and Radar Establishment</a>. In 1980, Bond was promoted to Superintendent of Computing and Software Research there.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As part of her role, Bond collaborated with the <a href="/wiki/Open_Software_Foundation" title="Open Software Foundation">Open Software Foundation</a> on an <a href="/wiki/International_standard" title="International standard">international</a> <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">open</a> <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standard</a> for <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> operating systems, named the <a href="/wiki/Architecture_Neutral_Distribution_Format" title="Architecture Neutral Distribution Format">Architecture Neutral Distribution Format</a>, and on computing policy for the UK <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defense</a>.
</p><p>Bond retired from work in 1993.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Susan_G._Bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bond met her husband, Chris Sennett, while working at the RRE.<sup id="cite_ref-OralHistory_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OralHistory-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
