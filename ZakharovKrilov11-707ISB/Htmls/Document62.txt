<p><b>L Peter Deutsch</b> (born <b>Laurence Peter Deutsch</b> on August 7, 1946, in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>) is the founder of Aladdin Enterprises and creator of <a href="/wiki/Ghostscript" title="Ghostscript">Ghostscript</a>, a free software <a href="/wiki/PostScript" title="PostScript">PostScript</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portable_Document_Format" class="mw-redirect" title="Portable Document Format">Portable Document Format</a> interpreter.
</p><p>Deutsch's other work includes the <a href="/wiki/Smalltalk" title="Smalltalk">Smalltalk</a> implementation that inspired <a href="/wiki/Java_(software_platform)" title="Java (software platform)">Java</a> <a href="/wiki/Just-in-time_compilation" title="Just-in-time compilation">just-in-time compilation</a> technology about 15 years later.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some stories about him are included in the <a href="/wiki/Hackers:_Heroes_of_the_Computer_Revolution" title="Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution">Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution</a> book.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_to_computer_science">Contributions to computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Peter_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contributions to computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He also wrote the <a href="/wiki/PDP-1" title="PDP-1">PDP-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language)" title="Lisp (programming language)">Lisp</a> 1.5 implementation, Basic PDP-1 LISP, "while still in short pants" and finished it in 1963, when he was 17 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1964 to 1967, during his study at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, he worked with <a href="/wiki/Butler_Lampson" title="Butler Lampson">Butler Lampson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_P._Thacker" title="Charles P. Thacker">Charles P. Thacker</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Timesharing_System" title="Berkeley Timesharing System">Berkeley Timesharing System</a>, which became the standard <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> for the <a href="/wiki/SDS_940" title="SDS 940">SDS 940</a> mainframe that would later be used by <a href="/wiki/Tymshare" title="Tymshare">Tymshare</a>, <a href="/wiki/NLS_(computer_system)" title="NLS (computer system)">NLS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Community_Memory" title="Community Memory">Community Memory</a>.
</p><p>Deutsch is the author of several <a href="/wiki/Request_for_Comments" title="Request for Comments">Request for Comments</a> (RFCs), <a href="/wiki/Fallacies_of_distributed_computing" title="Fallacies of distributed computing">The Eight Fallacies of Distributed Computing</a>, and originated the <a href="/wiki/Deutsch_limit" title="Deutsch limit">Deutsch limit</a> adage about <a href="/wiki/Visual_programming_language" title="Visual programming language">visual programming languages</a>.
</p><p>Deutsch received a Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> in 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and has previously worked at <a href="/wiki/PARC_(company)" title="PARC (company)">Xerox PARC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>. In 1994, he was inducted as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Association_for_Computing_Machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Fellows of the Association for Computing Machinery">Fellow of the Association for Computing Machinery</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._Peter_Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deutsch's father was the physicist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Deutsch" title="Martin Deutsch">Martin Deutsch</a>, a professor at MIT.
</p><p>Deutsch changed his legal first name from "Laurence" to "L" on September 12, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His published work and other public references before that time generally use the name <i>L. Peter Deutsch</i> (with a dot after the <i>L</i>).
</p><p>After auditing undergraduate music courses at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, in January 2009, he entered the postgraduate music program at <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_East_Bay" title="California State University, East Bay">California State University, East Bay</a>, and was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> (M.A.) in March 2011. As of mid-2011, he has had six compositions performed at public concerts, and now generally identifies himself as a composer rather than a software developer or engineer.
</p>
