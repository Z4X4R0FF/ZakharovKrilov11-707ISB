<p><b>Michael Abrash</b> is a software executive, <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technical_writer" title="Technical writer">technical writer</a> specializing in <a href="/wiki/Code_optimization" class="mw-redirect" title="Code optimization">code optimization</a> and <a href="/wiki/80x86" class="mw-redirect" title="80x86">80x86</a> <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly language</a>, a reputation cemented by his 1990 book <i>Zen of Assembly Language Volume 1: Knowledge</i><sup id="cite_ref-zenassembly_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zenassembly-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and a monthly column in <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Dobb%27s_Journal" title="Dr. Dobb&#39;s Journal">Dr. Dobb's Journal</a></i> in the early 1990s. A later book, <i>Zen of Graphics Programming</i>, applied these ideas to 2D and 3D graphics prior to the advent of hardware accelerators for the PC.<sup id="cite_ref-zengraphic_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zengraphic-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Though not strictly a game programmer, Abrash has worked on the underlying technology for games, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i>, for much of his career.  Since 2014, he has been the chief scientist of <a href="/wiki/Oculus_VR" class="mw-redirect" title="Oculus VR">Oculus VR</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook, Inc.</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oculus01_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oculus01-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He frequently begins a technical discussion with an anecdote that draws parallels between a real-life experience he has had and the article's subject matter. His prose encourages readers to <a href="/wiki/Think_outside_the_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Think outside the box">think outside the box</a> and to approach solving technical problems in an innovative way.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_programmer">Game programmer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Abrash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Game programmer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Abrash first bought a microcomputer while doing postgraduate studies at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Before getting into technical writing, Abrash was a <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Game programmer">game programmer</a> in the early days of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a>. His first commercial game was a clone of <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Invaders" title="Space Invaders">Space Invaders</a></i> published by <a href="/wiki/Datamost" title="Datamost">Datamost</a> in 1982 as <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Strike" title="Space Strike">Space Strike</a></i>.  He co-authored several PC games with Dan Illowsky, who had previously written the successful <i><a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a></i> clone <i><a href="/wiki/Snack_Attack" title="Snack Attack">Snack Attack</a></i> for the Apple II. Abrash and Illowsky worked together on the <i><a href="/wiki/Galaxian" title="Galaxian">Galaxian</a></i>-like <i><a href="/wiki/Cosmic_Crusader" title="Cosmic Crusader">Cosmic Crusader</a></i> (1982), maze game <i>Snack Attack II</i> (1982), and platform game <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Top_(video_game)" title="Big Top (video game)">Big Top</a></i> (1983).<sup id="cite_ref-giantlist_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giantlist-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After working at <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> on graphics and assembly code for <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT_3.1" title="Windows NT 3.1">Windows NT 3.1</a>, he returned to the <a href="/wiki/Video_game_industry" title="Video game industry">video game industry</a> in the mid-1990s to work on <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Id_Software" title="Id Software">id Software</a>. Some of the technology behind <i>Quake</i> is documented in Abrash's <i>Ramblings in Realtime</i> published in <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Dobb%27s_Journal" title="Dr. Dobb&#39;s Journal">Dr. Dobb's Journal</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-rir_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rir-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He mentions Quake as his favourite game of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After <i>Quake</i> was released, Abrash returned to Microsoft to work on natural language research, then moved to the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> team, until 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 2002, Abrash went to <a href="/wiki/RAD_Game_Tools" class="mw-redirect" title="RAD Game Tools">RAD Game Tools</a> where he co-wrote the Pixomatic <a href="/wiki/Software_rendering" title="Software rendering">software renderer</a>, which emulates the functionality of a <a href="/wiki/DirectX_7" class="mw-redirect" title="DirectX 7">DirectX 7</a>-level graphics card and is used as the software renderer in such games as <i><a href="/wiki/Unreal_Tournament_2004" title="Unreal Tournament 2004">Unreal Tournament 2004</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At the end of 2005, Pixomatic was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>. When developing Pixomatic, he and Mike Sartain designed a new architecture called <a href="/wiki/Larrabee_(microarchitecture)" title="Larrabee (microarchitecture)">Larrabee</a>, which now is part of Intel's <a href="/wiki/GPGPU" class="mw-redirect" title="GPGPU">GPGPU</a> project.<sup id="cite_ref-larrabee_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larrabee-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gabe_Newell" title="Gabe Newell">Gabe Newell</a>, managing director of <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">Valve</a>, said that he had "been trying to hire Michael Abrash forever. <i>[...]</i> About once a quarter we go for dinner and I say 'are you ready to work here yet?'"<sup id="cite_ref-gabenewell_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gabenewell-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2011 Abrash made the move to join Valve.<sup id="cite_ref-develop_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-develop-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 28, 2014, three days after <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> announced agreements to purchase the company,<sup id="cite_ref-Oculus022_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oculus022-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> headset company <a href="/wiki/Oculus_VR" class="mw-redirect" title="Oculus VR">Oculus VR</a> published a statement saying that Michael Abrash had joined their company as <a href="/wiki/Chief_science_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief science officer">Chief Scientist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oculus01_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oculus01-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_writer">Technical writer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Abrash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical writer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Abrash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Michael Abrash was a columnist in the 1980s for a magazine called <i>Programmer's Journal</i>. Those articles were collected in the 1989 book, <i>Power Graphics Programming</i>.
</p><p>His second book, <i>Zen of Assembly Language Volume 1: Knowledge</i> (1990), focused on writing efficient <a href="/wiki/Assembly_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Assembly code">assembly code</a> for the 16-bit 8086 processor, but was released after the <a href="/wiki/80486" class="mw-redirect" title="80486">80486</a> CPU was already available.<sup id="cite_ref-jameshague_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jameshague-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In addition to assembly-level optimization, the book focused on parts of the system that silently affect code performance, which he called "cycle eaters." A key point of <i>Zen of Assembly Language</i> is that performance must always be measured, and the book included the "Zen Timer" tool to check if theoretical code optimizations actually worked. Volume 2 was never published.
</p>
<h3><span id="Dr._Dobb.27s_and_later_books"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dr._Dobb's_and_later_books">Dr. Dobb's and later books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Abrash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dr. Dobb&#039;s and later books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early to mid-1990s, Abrash wrote a PC graphics programming column for <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Dobb%27s_Journal" title="Dr. Dobb&#39;s Journal">Dr. Dobb's Journal</a></i> called "Ramblings in Realtime." In 1991 he introduced <a href="/wiki/Mode_X" title="Mode X">Mode X</a>, a 320x240 VGA graphics mode with square pixels instead of the slightly elongated pixels of the standard 320x200 mode. At the same time, he introduced readers to a little known part of the VGA standard allowing multiple pixels to be written at once. The article and its follow-ups ignited interest among PC game programmers.
</p><p>Much of the content of <i>Zen of Assembly Language</i> was updated in <i>Zen of Code Optimization: The Ultimate Guide to Writing Software That Pushes PCs to the Limit</i> (1994),<sup id="cite_ref-zen_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> along with new material. The presentation of stepwise program refinement empirically demonstrated how algorithm re-design could improve performance up to a factor of 100. Assembly language re-coding, on the other hand, may only improve performance by a factor of 10. Abrash also showed how elusive performance improvement can be, and improving performance in one subroutine can expose bottlenecks in other routines. Finally, he demonstrated processor-dependent assembly-based performance improvements by comparing assembly language optimizations across x86 generations, including how some micro-optimizations disappeared or even made a program slower.
</p><p>In 1997 Abrash's <i>Graphics Programming Black Book</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-gpbb-book_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpbb-book-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gpbb_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpbb-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> was published. It was a collection of his <i>Dr. Dobb's Journal</i> articles and his work on the <i><a href="/wiki/Quake_(video_game)" title="Quake (video game)">Quake</a></i> graphic subsystem.
</p><p>Abrash stopped writing publicly in the 2000s until maintaining a public blog at Valve, "Ramblings in Valve Time," from April 2012 until January 2014.
</p>
