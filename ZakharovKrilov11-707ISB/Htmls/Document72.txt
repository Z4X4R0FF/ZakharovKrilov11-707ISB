<p><a href="/wiki/Academician" title="Academician">Academician</a> <b>Andrey Petrovych Ershov</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Андре́й Петро́вич Ершо́в</span>, <i>Andrey Petrovich Yershov</i>; 19 April 1931, <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> – 8 December 1988, Moscow) was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, notable as a pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Systems_programming" title="Systems programming">systems programming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> research.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Donald_Knuth" title="Donald Knuth">Donald Knuth</a> considers him to have independently codiscovered the idea of <a href="/wiki/Hash_function" title="Hash function">hashing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Linear_probing" title="Linear probing">linear probing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He also created one of the first algorithms for compiling arithmetic expressions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>He was responsible for the languages <i>ALPHA</i><sup id="cite_ref-archive_bio_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive_bio-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rapira" title="Rapira">Rapira</a></i>, the first Soviet <a href="/wiki/Time-sharing" title="Time-sharing">time-sharing</a> system <i>AIST-0</i>, electronic publishing system <i>RUBIN</i>, and a <a href="/wiki/Multiprocessing" title="Multiprocessing">multiprocessing</a> <a href="/wiki/Workstation" title="Workstation">workstation</a> <i>MRAMOR</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-achives_index_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-achives_index-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He also was the initiator of developing the <i>Computer Bank of the Russian Language</i> (<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9C%D0%B0%D1%88%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%BD%D1%8B%D0%B9_%D1%84%D0%BE%D0%BD%D0%B4_%D1%80%D1%83%D1%81%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B3%D0%BE_%D1%8F%D0%B7%D1%8B%D0%BA%D0%B0" class="extiw" title="ru:Машинный фонд русского языка">Машинный Фонд Русского Языка</a>), the Soviet project for creating a large representative Russian <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">corpus</a>, a project in the 1980s comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_English" title="Bank of English">Bank of English</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_National_Corpus" title="British National Corpus">British National Corpus</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_National_Corpus" title="Russian National Corpus">Russian National Corpus</a> created by the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a> in the 2000s is a successor of Ershov's project.
</p><p>From 1959, he worked at the Siberian Division of the <a href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">USSR Academy of Sciences</a>, and helped found both the <i>Novosibirsk Computer Center</i> and the <i>Siberian School of Computer Science</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-achives_index_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-achives_index-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He received the <a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9F%D1%80%D0%B5%D0%BC%D0%B8%D1%8F_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B8_%D0%90._%D0%9D._%D0%9A%D1%80%D1%8B%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0" class="extiw" title="ru:Премия имени А. Н. Крылова">Academician A. N. Krylov Prize</a> from the Academy of Sciences, the first programmer to be so recognized.<sup id="cite_ref-archive_bio_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive_bio-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1974, he was made a Distinguished Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a>.
</p><p>He was involved with developing <a href="/wiki/International_standard" title="International standard">international standards</a> in programming and informatics, as a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Information_Processing" title="International Federation for Information Processing">International Federation for Information Processing</a> (IFIP) <a href="/wiki/IFIP_Working_Group_2.1" title="IFIP Working Group 2.1">IFIP Working Group 2.1</a> on Algorithmic Languages and Calculi,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specified</a>, maintains, and supports the languages <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_60" title="ALGOL 60">ALGOL 60</a> and <a href="/wiki/ALGOL_68" title="ALGOL 68">ALGOL 68</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1981, he received the IFIP's <i>Silver Core Award</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-achives_index_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-achives_index-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To the computer science community, he is mostly known for his speech <i>Aesthetics and the Human Factor in Programming</i> presented at the dinner at the AFIPS Spring Joint Computer Conference in 1972<sup id="cite_ref-achives_index_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-achives_index-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and, due to its importance, republished as an article by the <a href="/wiki/Communications_of_the_ACM" title="Communications of the ACM">Communications of the ACM</a>.
</p>
