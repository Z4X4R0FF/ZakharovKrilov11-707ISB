<p><b>Leonard Adleman</b> (born December 31, 1945) is an American computer scientist. He is one of the creators of the <a href="/wiki/RSA_(cryptosystem)" title="RSA (cryptosystem)">RSA</a> encryption algorithm, for which he received the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a>, often called the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">Computer science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He is also known for the creation of the field of <a href="/wiki/DNA_computing" title="DNA computing">DNA computing</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonard_Adleman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leonard M. Adleman was born to a <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a><sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  family in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. His family had originally immigrated to the United States from modern-day <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Minsk" title="Minsk">Minsk</a> area.<sup id="cite_ref-amturing.acm.org_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amturing.acm.org-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He grew up in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> and attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, where he received his <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">BA</a> degree in mathematics in 1968 and his <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_Engineering_and_Computer_Science" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical Engineering and Computer Science">EECS</a> in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was also the mathematical consultant on the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Sneakers_(1992_film)" title="Sneakers (1992 film)">Sneakers</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He is a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Engineering" title="National Academy of Engineering">National Academy of Engineering</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adleman is also an amateur boxer and has sparred with <a href="/wiki/James_Toney" title="James Toney">James Toney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonard_Adleman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1994, his paper <i>Molecular Computation of Solutions To Combinatorial Problems</i> described the experimental use of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> as a computational system.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In it, he solved a seven-node instance of the <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_path" title="Hamiltonian path">Hamiltonian Graph</a> problem, an <a href="/wiki/NP-complete" class="mw-redirect" title="NP-complete">NP-complete</a> problem similar to the <a href="/wiki/Travelling_salesman_problem" title="Travelling salesman problem">travelling salesman problem</a>. While the solution to a seven-node instance is <a href="/wiki/Trivial_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trivial (mathematics)">trivial</a>, this paper is the first known instance of the successful use of DNA to compute an <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a>. DNA computing has been shown to have potential as a means to solve several other large-scale combinatorial search problems.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Adleman is widely referred to as the Father of DNA Computing.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, he and his research group managed to solve a 'nontrivial' problem using DNA computation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Specifically, they solved a 20-variable <a href="/wiki/Boolean_satisfiability_problem" title="Boolean satisfiability problem">SAT</a> problem having more than 1 million potential solutions.  They did it in a manner similar to the one Adleman used in his seminal 1994 paper.  First, a mixture of DNA strands logically representative of the problem's solution space was synthesized. This mixture was then operated upon algorithmically using biochemical techniques to winnow out the 'incorrect' strands, leaving behind only those strands that 'satisfied' the problem.  Analysis of the nucleotide sequence of these remaining strands revealed 'correct' solutions to the original problem.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is one of the original discoverers of the <a href="/wiki/Adleman%E2%80%93Pomerance%E2%80%93Rumely_primality_test" title="Adleman–Pomerance–Rumely primality test">Adleman–Pomerance–Rumely primality test</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fred_Cohen" title="Fred Cohen">Fred Cohen</a>, in his 1984 paper, <i>Experiments with Computer Viruses</i> credited Adleman with coining the term "<a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">computer virus</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>As of 2017, Adleman is working on the mathematical theory of Strata, however, no results have been disclosed, and a wikipedia search reveals that there are no other references to such a 'theory of strata', even though it would be interesting if it had been elaborated. He is a Computer Science professor at the University of Southern California.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonard_Adleman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For his contribution to the invention of the <a href="/wiki/RSA_(algorithm)" class="mw-redirect" title="RSA (algorithm)">RSA</a> cryptosystem, Adleman, along with <a href="/wiki/Ron_Rivest" title="Ron Rivest">Ron Rivest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shamir" title="Adi Shamir">Adi Shamir</a>, has been a recipient of the 1996 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Kanellakis_Theory_and_Practice_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Kanellakis Theory and Practice Award">Paris Kanellakis Theory and Practice Award</a> and the 2002 <a href="/wiki/ACM_Turing_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="ACM Turing Award">ACM Turing Award</a>, often called the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> of Computer Science.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Adleman was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-AAAS_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAAS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
