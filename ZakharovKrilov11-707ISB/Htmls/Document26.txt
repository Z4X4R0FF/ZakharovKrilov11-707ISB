<p><b>Jonathan David Blow</b> (born <span style="display:none">(<span class="bday">1971-11-03</span>)</span>November 3, 1971)<sup id="cite_ref-born_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-born-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Video_game_designer" class="mw-redirect" title="Video game designer">video game designer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Game_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Game programmer">programmer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twitch_(service)" title="Twitch (service)">Twitch</a> streamer, who is best known as the creator of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_video_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent video game">independent video games</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Braid_(video_game)" title="Braid (video game)">Braid</a></i> (2008) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Witness_(2016_video_game)" title="The Witness (2016 video game)">The Witness</a></i> (2016), both of which were released to critical acclaim.
</p><p>From 2001 to 2004, Blow wrote the Inner Product column for <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Developer_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Game Developer Magazine">Game Developer Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was the primary host of the Experimental Gameplay Workshop each March at the <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">Game Developers Conference</a>, which has become a premier showcase for new ideas in video games.  In addition, Blow was a regular participant in the <a href="/wiki/Indie_Game_Jam" title="Indie Game Jam">Indie Game Jam</a>. Blow is also a founding partner of the <a href="/wiki/Indie_Fund" title="Indie Fund">Indie Fund</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investor</a> fund for independent game projects.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blow was born in 1971. His mother was a devout ex-nun. Blow's father, an aerospace engineer manager, worked for the defense contractor TRW. Blow would say in an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i>, "Early on, I detected that there weren't good examples at home, so I kind of had to figure things out on my own ... I had to adopt a paradigm of self-sufficiency."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blow grew up in <a href="/wiki/La_Palma,_California" title="La Palma, California">La Palma</a> until he was 8, then he moved to <a href="/wiki/Rancho_Pe%C3%B1asquitos,_San_Diego" title="Rancho Peñasquitos, San Diego">Rancho Peñasquitos, San Diego</a>, where he attended <a href="/wiki/Mt._Carmel_High_School_(San_Diego)" title="Mt. Carmel High School (San Diego)">Mt. Carmel High School</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. He then studied <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creative_writing" title="Creative writing">creative writing</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> and was president of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_Science_Undergraduate_Association" title="Computer Science Undergraduate Association">Computer Science Undergraduate Association</a> for a semester. He left the university in 1994, a semester before he would have graduated.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-arstech_thewitness_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_thewitness-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He worked in San Francisco in contracting jobs, including one with <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">Silicon Graphics</a> to port <i><a href="/wiki/Doom_(1993_video_game)" title="Doom (1993 video game)">Doom</a></i> to a set-top device, until forming the game design company Bolt-Action Software with Bernt Habermeier in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-arstech_thewitness_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_thewitness-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their initial game project was to be a hovertank-based combat game called <i>Wulfram</i>, but at the time, the video game industry was undergoing a transformation of focusing heavily on three-dimensional graphics, making it difficult for them to complete the project;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="It&#39;s never stated that Wulfram is not 3D. (June 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the team was forced to take some online database work to cover their expenditures. Subsequently, in the wake of the crash of <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, they opted to fold the business after four years in 2000, with them $100,000 in debt.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-arstech_thewitness_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_thewitness-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following Bolt-Action, Blow continued to perform contract work for companies like <a href="/wiki/Ion_Storm" title="Ion Storm">Ion Storm</a>, and writing for industry publications such as <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Developer_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Game Developer Magazine">Game Developer Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-arstech_thewitness_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_thewitness-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He also worked on a project with <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> to create a technology demo similar to the <i>Wulfram</i> idea that would highlight the features of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(microprocessor)" title="Cell (microprocessor)">Cell processor</a> that IBM was collaborating on, which would become a part of the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a>. Blow attempted to get additional funding to turn the demo into a full game from both <a href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a> but was unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-arstech_thewitness_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arstech_thewitness-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jonathan_Blow,_Gamelab_2018_(42432130094).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg/220px-Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg/330px-Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg/440px-Jonathan_Blow%2C_Gamelab_2018_%2842432130094%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jonathan_Blow,_Gamelab_2018_(42432130094).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blow in 2018</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Braid"><i>Braid</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Braid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Braid_(video_game)" title="Braid (video game)">Braid (video game)</a></div>
<p>In December 2004, feeling inspired during a trip in Thailand, Blow made a prototype for a time manipulation puzzle platformer game. The demo had crude graphics, but featured the ability of the player to rewind all the objects on screen backwards in time to a previous state. Encouraged by feedback from his peers, Blow worked on the game from about April 2005 to about December that year before having the final prototype of his game, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Braid_(video_game)" title="Braid (video game)">Braid</a></i>. This version won the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Games_Festival" title="Independent Games Festival">Independent Games Festival</a> Game Design Award at the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Game_Developers_Conference" title="Game Developers Conference">Game Developers Conference</a>.
</p><p>He continued work on the game mostly focusing on art and music while polishing some of the design until its release in 2008 on <a href="/wiki/Xbox_Live_Arcade" title="Xbox Live Arcade">Xbox Live Arcade</a>. By then, Blow was $40,000 in debt<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and had invested $200,000 into the game's development.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The game was released on August 8, 2008, to critical acclaim and achieved financial success, receiving an aggregate score of 93% on <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, making it the top-rated Xbox Live Arcade game. <i>Braid</i> was purchased by more than 55,000 people during the first week of release.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Blow recalled that he did not receive any money until some time later when he suddenly saw "a lot of zeroes" in his bank account.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Witness"><i>The Witness</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Witness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Witness_(2016_video_game)" title="The Witness (2016 video game)">The Witness (2016 video game)</a></div>
<p>Announced in August 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Witness</i> is a 3D first person <a href="/wiki/Puzzle_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Puzzle game">puzzle game</a> in which a player is stranded on an island, trying to solve various maze puzzles. Like <i>Braid</i> before it, Blow invested his own money—reportedly $2–3&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early public reaction to preview footage resulted in underwhelming assumptions that the game would simply be "solving simple maze puzzles." Blow responded by saying that footage does not capture the problem-solving process that goes on in the player's mind like in his previous game <i>Braid</i>, and that he "wouldn't make a game about solving a series of rote puzzles."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In previews of <i>The Witness</i> (often at noisy conventions), Blow had journalists play the game by themselves in a quiet environment so as to fit the tone of the game's design. There was praise of the game's previews,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> notably Kirk Hamilton from <a href="/wiki/Kotaku" title="Kotaku">Kotaku</a> calling it "an exercise in Symphonic Game Design."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blow reinvested all of his remaining profit from <i>Braid</i> into <i>The Witness,</i> and had to borrow funds when his own ran out.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jai_language">Jai language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jai language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks fmbox fmbox-editnotice" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg/40px-Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="34" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg/60px-Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg/80px-Ambox_warning_green_construction.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="378" data-file-height="326" /></td><td class="mbox-text">There is a draft for this article at <a href="/wiki/Draft:Jai_(programming_language)" title="Draft:Jai (programming language)">Draft:Jai (programming language)</a>.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In September 2014, Blow began work on a new <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming language</a> called "Jai" based on ideas he had previously expressed about <a href="/wiki/Video_game_development" title="Video game development">video game development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is aimed at "low-friction" and fluid development, while directly supporting <a href="/wiki/Data-oriented_design" title="Data-oriented design">data-oriented design</a> for high performance. The language is currently active, but is a work-in-progress, as no compiler for it has been publicly released yet. A small demo of the language was shown at Reboot Develop 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Blow also uses <a href="/wiki/Twitch_(service)" title="Twitch (service)">Twitch</a> to regularly stream demonstrations of new language features as well as improvements to the game engine that will be bundled with the language.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_3"><i>Game 3</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Game 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blow's next project is being built with the Jai language and allows the player to guide characters through a variety of levels to solve puzzles.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Game_4"><i>Game 4</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Game 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2012 or 2013, Blow has been working on a separate project that will be broken into different installments and elaborations on the same game over the course of 20 years, making it bigger and more complex. They will be individual playable games, each related thematically and deepening in investigation of subject matter for each chapter. Blow stated that the game would not be puzzle-related and that it would be built with the Jai language and engine.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2010, Blow, along with several independent game developers including Ron Carmel and <a href="/wiki/Kellee_Santiago" title="Kellee Santiago">Kellee Santiago</a>, became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Indie_Fund" title="Indie Fund">Indie Fund</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Angel_investor" title="Angel investor">angel investor</a> fund for independent game projects.
</p><p>In 2012, Blow was one of the subjects of the independent documentary film, <i><a href="/wiki/Indie_Game:_The_Movie" title="Indie Game: The Movie">Indie Game: The Movie</a></i>, where he discussed his views on the role of independent video games and his work on <i>Braid</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_and_views">Philosophy and views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Blow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Philosophy and views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blow has spoken many times about his views on independent video games both in interviews and in public speeches, although he has said on his blog<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> that he has gotten what he wanted out of conferences from speaking at them. For his sometimes controversial views, he has received praise, notably being called "the kind of righteous rebel video games need"<sup id="cite_ref-kotaku.com_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kotaku.com-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and "a spiritual seeker, questing after truth in an as-yet-uncharted realm."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Most_Dangerous_Gamer-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blow often speaks of the potential for games to be more. He has said that he tries to make games that are more adult for people with longer attention spans<sup id="cite_ref-kotaku.com_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kotaku.com-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and noted that games could have a "much bigger role" in culture in the future, but current game development does not address this potential, instead aiming for low-risk, high-profit titles.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, formerly being a physics major, Blow has expressed that games could examine the universe in similar ways that a physicist could.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In referring to the progression of development in his games, Blow stated at the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_Experience" title="PlayStation Experience">PlayStation Experience</a> that he prefers to, "keep them playable and just make them better." This was stated during the live-cast panel while overseeing Justin Massongill on the playable demo.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blow has spoken out against some games for immoral game design. On <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_Warcraft" title="World of Warcraft">World of Warcraft</a></i>, he has said it causes societal problems by creating a false image of the meaning of life, calling it "unethical."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> On <i><a href="/wiki/FarmVille" title="FarmVille">FarmVille</a></i>, he has said that the design of the game reveals the developers' goal to degrade the quality of players' lives, ultimately calling it "inherently evil."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite <i>Braid</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s success on the platform, Blow has claimed that <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s Xbox Live Arcade certification process would turn away developers because "they kind of make themselves a pain in the ass" and that they would lose <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a> to <a href="/wiki/Steam_(service)" title="Steam (service)">Steam</a> as a result.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blow is a member of <a href="/wiki/Giving_What_We_Can" title="Giving What We Can">Giving What We Can</a>, a community of people who have pledged to give at least 10% of their income to <a href="/wiki/Effective_altruism" title="Effective altruism">effective charities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
