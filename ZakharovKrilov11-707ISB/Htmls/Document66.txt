<p><b>Jack Patrick Dorsey</b> (born November 19, 1976)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Internet_Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Entrepreneur">technology entrepreneur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philanthropy" title="Philanthropy">philanthropist</a> who is a co-founder and the CEO of <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, and the founder and CEO of <a href="/wiki/Square,_Inc." title="Square, Inc.">Square</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_payment" title="Mobile payment">financial payments</a> company.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>Dorsey was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the son of Tim and Marcia (née Smith) Dorsey.<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He is of <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> descent.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His father worked for a company that developed mass <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometers</a> and his mother was a homemaker.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He was raised <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a>, and his uncle is a Catholic priest in Cincinnati.<sup id="cite_ref-native_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He attended the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Bishop_DuBourg_High_School" title="Bishop DuBourg High School">Bishop DuBourg High School</a>. In his younger days, Dorsey worked occasionally as a fashion model.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> By age 14, Dorsey had become interested in <a href="/wiki/Dispatch_(logistics)" title="Dispatch (logistics)">dispatch routing</a>. Some of the <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open-source</a> software he created in the area of dispatch logistics is still used by taxicab companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Dorsey attended the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_University_of_Science_and_Technology" title="Missouri University of Science and Technology">University of Missouri–Rolla</a> for two-plus years (1995–97)<sup id="cite_ref-native_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> before transferring to <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, but he dropped out in 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> one semester short of graduating.<sup id="cite_ref-native_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He came up with the idea that he developed as <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> while studying at NYU.<sup id="cite_ref-native_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While working on dispatching as a programmer, Dorsey moved to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BW_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BW-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dorseytweet_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dorseytweet-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, Dorsey started his company in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a> to dispatch couriers, taxis, and emergency services from the Web.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorsey_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorsey-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> His other projects and ideas at this time included networks of medical devices and a "frictionless service market".<sup id="cite_ref-Dorsey_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorsey-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In July 2000, building on dispatching<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and inspired in part by <a href="/wiki/LiveJournal" title="LiveJournal">LiveJournal</a> and by <a href="/wiki/AIM_(software)" title="AIM (software)">AOL Instant Messenger</a>, he had the idea for a Web-based realtime status/short message communication service.<sup id="cite_ref-Dorsey_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dorsey-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When he first saw implementations of <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a>, Dorsey wondered whether the software's user status output could be shared easily among friends.<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He approached <a href="/wiki/Odeo" title="Odeo">Odeo</a>, which at the time happened to be interested in <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">text messaging</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Dorsey and <a href="/wiki/Biz_Stone" title="Biz Stone">Biz Stone</a> decided that SMS text suited the status-message idea, and built a prototype of <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> in about two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The idea attracted many users at Odeo and investment from <a href="/wiki/Evan_Williams_(Internet_entrepreneur)" title="Evan Williams (Internet entrepreneur)">Evan Williams</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> a co-founder of that firm in 2005 who had left <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> after selling <a href="/wiki/Pyra_Labs" title="Pyra Labs">Pyra Labs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blogger_(service)" title="Blogger (service)">Blogger</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twitter">Twitter</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg/220px-Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg/330px-Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg/440px-Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3916" data-file-height="2634" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jack_Dorsey-20080723.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dorsey in 2008</div></div></div>
<p>Williams, Stone and <a href="/wiki/Noah_Glass_(Twitter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Noah Glass (Twitter)">Noah Glass</a> co-founded Obvious Corporation, which then spun off Twitter, Inc., with Dorsey as the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">Chief Executive Officer</a> (CEO).<sup id="cite_ref-Glaser_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glaser-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> As CEO, Dorsey saw the startup through two rounds of funding by venture capitalists.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He reportedly lost his position for leaving work early to enjoy other pursuits, such as yoga and fashion design.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the service began to grow in popularity, Dorsey chose the improvement of uptime as top priority,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> even over creating revenue—which, as of 2008, Twitter was not designed to earn.<sup id="cite_ref-Wagner-2_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-2-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Dorsey described the commercial use of Twitter and its API as two things that could lead to paid features.<sup id="cite_ref-Wagner-2_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-2-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He says his three guiding principles, which he says the company shares, are simplicity, constraint and craftsmanship.<sup id="cite_ref-Wagner-2_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-2-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 16, 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Williams took over as CEO, while Dorsey became chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> During his time as chairman, Dorsey joined several State Department delegations, including a trip to Iraq in April 2009, led by <a href="/wiki/Jared_Cohen" title="Jared Cohen">Jared Cohen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In November, when Iranians took to the streets in the <a href="/wiki/2009_Iranian_presidential_election_protests" title="2009 Iranian presidential election protests">Green Revolution</a>, Twitter was scheduled to conduct maintenance of its site, which would entail temporarily shutting down Twitter's servers. Dorsey responded to a request from Cohen to delay the maintenance so that it would not affect the revolution in Iran, because Iranians were using Twitter to communicate and coordinate.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Since President Obama had announced that there would be no meddling in Iran, the move sparked controversy.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In February 2010, Dorsey was part of another State Department delegation, this time to Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> On March 28, 2011, he returned to Twitter as executive chairman after <a href="/wiki/Dick_Costolo" title="Dick Costolo">Dick Costolo</a> replaced Williams as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-post_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-post-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> On June 10, 2015, Costolo announced his resignation as CEO, effective July 1, 2015. Dorsey assumed the post of interim CEO upon Costolo's departure.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> He was named permanent CEO on October 5, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> On the day after the controversy about Twitter's new algorithms for tweets, Dorsey said it was a hoax.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In May 2016, Dorsey announced that Twitter would not count photos and links in the 140-character limit to free up more space for text. This was an attempt to entice new users, since the number of tweets per day had dropped to about 300 million in January 2016 from about 500 million in September 2013 and its peak of 661 million in August 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 22, 2016, Dorsey was briefly suspended from his own Twitter account with 3.9 million followers. After restoring the account, Dorsey tweeted that the suspension was due to an "internal mistake".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2017, Dorsey and Executive Chairman <a href="/wiki/Omid_Kordestani" title="Omid Kordestani">Omid Kordestani</a> matched a $530,000 donation to the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> (ACLU) raised by Twitter staffers. Their match brought the total donation to $1.59 million.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2018, Dorsey announced that an improved version of the <a href="/wiki/Identity_verification_service" title="Identity verification service">verification system</a> would be coming to Twitter. The purpose of redesigning verification was to let people verify more facts about themselves, emphasizing proof of identity.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The overhaul was not in place before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_midterm_election" title="United States midterm election">U.S midterm election</a> of <a href="/wiki/2018_United_States_elections" title="2018 United States elections">2018</a> to help in <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">verifying the identities</a> of the candidates.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2018, Dorsey testified before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Select_Committee_on_Intelligence" title="United States Senate Select Committee on Intelligence">Senate Intelligence Committee</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">COO</a> <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Sandberg" title="Sheryl Sandberg">Sheryl Sandberg</a> about meddling in the <a href="/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election" title="2016 United States presidential election">2016 presidential election</a>. Following this testimony, Twitter shares fell six percent.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_(40715975903).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg/220px-President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg/330px-President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg/440px-President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_%2840715975903%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Trump_Meets_with_Twitter_CEO_Jack_Dorsey_(40715975903).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> with Dorsey in the Oval Office of the White House on April 23, 2019</div></div></div>
<p>Dorsey met privately with U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> at the White House and discussed Trump's concerns that Twitter had <a href="/wiki/Shadow_banning" title="Shadow banning">limited or removed</a> some of his Twitter followers, and those of conservatives. After the meeting, Dorsey tweeted that their discussion included making Twitter "healthier and more civil".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> A week earlier, Dorsey took part in a <a href="/wiki/TED_(conference)" title="TED (conference)">TED talk</a> about the social media platform's spread of abuse and misinformation, which has brought him criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 30, 2019, Dorsey's personal Twitter account was allegedly breached for nearly an hour by a group calling itself the Chuckling Squad, posting and retweeting numerous racist tweets.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 23, 2019, Twitter's stock price fell by nearly 24 percent, from $38.83 to $30.75. The reason was an earnings miss off a third quarter report, which Twitter blamed on ad targeting problems. Dorsey had been making a concerted effort to dampen the effect that Twitter had on political elections, which entailed banning all political ads. This was also seen as a large contributor to the drop. Dorsey announced that, as of November 22, 2019, Twitter would ban all political advertising. The policy applies globally to all marketing campaigns about political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 29, 2020, it was announced<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> that activist hedge fund <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Management_Corporation" title="Elliott Management Corporation">Elliott Management</a> led by billionaire <a href="/wiki/Paul_Singer_(businessman)" title="Paul Singer (businessman)">Paul Singer</a> was looking to oust Dorsey and nominate four directors to Twitter's board, including Elliott's senior portfolio manager Jesse Cohn.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Dorsey received support from entrepreneurs <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> and Ethereum co-founder <a href="/wiki/Vitalik_Buterin" title="Vitalik Buterin">Vitalik Buterin</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The two parties reached an agreement days later, with Dorsey remaining CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2020, Dorsey was one of several tech firm CEOs subpoenaed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Commerce,_Science,_and_Transportation" title="United States Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation">US Senate Commerce Committee</a>. <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Wicker" title="Roger Wicker">Roger Wicker</a>, who chairs the committee, led the charge to force the CEOs of Twitter, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> to testify about the legal immunity the tech platforms receive under <a href="/wiki/Section_230" title="Section 230">Section 230 of the Communications Act</a> of 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Square.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Square,_Inc.">Square, Inc.</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MRW_4445_(44538272181).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg/220px-MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg/330px-MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg/440px-MRW_4445_%2844538272181%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5844" data-file-height="3901" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MRW_4445_(44538272181).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dorsey in 2018</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Square,_Inc." title="Square, Inc.">Square, Inc.</a></div>
<p>Dorsey, along with co-founder <a href="/wiki/Jim_McKelvey" title="Jim McKelvey">Jim McKelvey</a>, developed a small business platform to accept debit and credit card payments on a mobile device called <a href="/wiki/Square,_Inc." title="Square, Inc.">Square</a>, released in May 2010. The small, square-shaped device attaches to <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPod_Touch" title="IPod Touch">iPod Touch</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> devices via the headphone jack, and as a mini card reader, allows a person to swipe their card, choose an amount to transfer to the recipient and then sign their name for confirmation. Square is also a system for sending paperless receipts via text message or email, and is available as a free app for iOS and Android OS.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The company grew from 10 employees in December 2009<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> to over 100 by June 2011. Square's office is on Market Street in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> In September 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/Business_Insider" title="Business Insider">Business Insider</a></i> magazine valued Square Inc. at <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>3.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Dorsey is CEO of Square, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-Jack_Dorsey_Looks_to_Simplify_Commerce_With_Square_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jack_Dorsey_Looks_to_Simplify_Commerce_With_Square-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> On October 14, 2015, Square filed for an IPO to be listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> As of that date, Dorsey owned 24.4 percent of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In March 2020 the <a href="/wiki/FDIC" class="mw-redirect" title="FDIC">FDIC</a> permitted Square to open a bank. It announced plans to launch Square Financial Services in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2020, Dorsey extended to Square employees the same offer he made to those at Twitter, the option to work from home permanently.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, Square began withholding for months up to 30 percent of the funds that merchants collected from customers using its <a href="/wiki/Cash_App" title="Cash App">Cash App</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span></h3>
<p>In 2019, Dorsey contributed financially to the campaigns of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> 2020 presidential candidates <a href="/wiki/Tulsi_Gabbard" title="Tulsi Gabbard">Tulsi Gabbard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Yang" title="Andrew Yang">Andrew Yang</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, he donated $15 million to 29 mayors pursuing the piloting of <a href="/wiki/Guaranteed_basic_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Guaranteed basic income">guaranteed basic income</a> programs in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until 2021, Dorsey applied "world leader" exceptions that enabled President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> to post content on Twitter that would normally be removed or generate sanctions per the platform's rules. In May 2020, some of Trump's tweets received warning labels, and from Election Day in November 2020, more flaggings were applied to his tweets.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> On January 6, 2021, after pro-Trump supporters <a href="/wiki/2021_storming_of_the_United_States_Capitol" title="2021 storming of the United States Capitol">stormed the U.S. Capitol</a>, Twitter applied a 12-hour timeout to Trump's account for violating its Civic Integrity policy. Trump's account was suspended permanently on January 8.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> On January 14, Dorsey defended banning Trump, but also said it "sets a precedent I feel is dangerous."<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Kendi">Ibram X. Kendi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
