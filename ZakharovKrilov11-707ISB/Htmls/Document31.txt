	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >David Bradley (engineer)</h1>
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>

		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">American academic and engineer</div>
<p><b>David J. Bradley</b> (born 4 January 1949) is one of the twelve engineers who worked on the original <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a>, developing the computer's <a href="/wiki/ROM_BIOS" class="mw-redirect" title="ROM BIOS">ROM BIOS</a> code. Bradley is credited for implementing the "<a href="/wiki/Control-Alt-Delete" title="Control-Alt-Delete">Control-Alt-Delete</a>" (Ctrl-Alt-Del) key combination that was used to <a href="/wiki/Booting" title="Booting">reboot</a> the computer. Bradley joined IBM in 1975 after earning his doctorate in electrical engineering from Purdue University with a dissertation on computer architectures. &#160;
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Bradley_(engineer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bachelors, Electrical Engineering, University of Dayton (Ohio), 1971.
Master of Science, Electrical Engineering, Purdue University, 1972.
PhD, Electrical Engineering, Purdue University, 1975.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control-Alt-Delete">Control-Alt-Delete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Bradley_(engineer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Control-Alt-Delete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Bradley, <a href="/wiki/Control-Alt-Delete" title="Control-Alt-Delete">Control-Alt-Delete</a> was not intended to be used by end users, originally&#8212;it was meant to be used by people writing programs or documentation, so that they could reboot their computers without powering them down.  This was useful since after a computer was powered down, it was necessary to wait a few seconds before powering it up again to avoid potential damage to the <a href="/wiki/Power_supply_unit_(computer)" title="Power supply unit (computer)">power supply</a> and hard drive.  Since software developers and technical writers would need to restart a computer many times, this key combination was a big time-saver.  David Bradley and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mel_Hallerman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mel Hallerman (page does not exist)">Mel Hallerman</a> chose this key combination because it is practically impossible to accidentally press this combination of keys on a standard original IBM PC keyboard.<sup id="cite_ref-Aamidor_2003_CtrlAltDel_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aamidor_2003_CtrlAltDel-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the key combination was described in IBM's technical reference documentation<sup id="cite_ref-IBM_PC_Technical-Reference_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBM_PC_Technical-Reference-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and thereby revealed to the general public.
</p><p>At the 20th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> on August 8, 2001 at <a href="/wiki/The_Tech_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="The Tech Museum">The Tech Museum</a>, while on a panel with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a>, Bradley said, <i>"I have to share the credit.  I may have invented it [Control-Alt-Delete], but I think Bill made it famous."</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Orlowski_2004_Bradley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orlowski_2004_Bradley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple-key reboot had been introduced by Exidy, Inc., in 1978, for its <a href="/wiki/Exidy_Sorcerer" title="Exidy Sorcerer">Sorcerer</a> Z80 computer.  It provided two Reset buttons, which must be pressed simultaneously to achieve reboot.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 1980, the multiple-key reboot concept had been introduced for the Apple II by Videx in its VideoTerm display card add-on, requiring Control-Reset, rather than Reset alone, to reboot the machine.  The innovation was noted and well received at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>Bradley holds seven U.S. patents.
</p><p>Bradley has been adjunct professor of electrical and computer engineering at <a href="/wiki/Florida_Atlantic_University" title="Florida Atlantic University">Florida Atlantic University</a> and at <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_University" title="North Carolina State University">North Carolina State University</a>.
</p><p>Much of Bradley's career has been at <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>.  Bradley received a B.E.E. degree in 1971 from the University of Dayton, (Ohio). He went on to Purdue University in West Lafayette, Indiana, where he completed an M.S. degree in 1972 and  Ph.D. in 1975, both in electrical engineering.  Upon graduation he went to work for IBM in Boca Raton, Florida, as senior associate engineer. He worked on the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Series/1" title="IBM Series/1">Series/1</a> system.  In 1978 he developed the <a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">I/O</a> system for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_System/23_Datamaster" title="IBM System/23 Datamaster">System/23 Datamaster</a>.
</p><p>In 1980 Bradley was one of twelve engineers developing the first IBM Personal Computer. Bradley developed the <a href="/wiki/Read-only_memory" title="Read-only memory">ROM</a> <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a>.   That got him promoted to manage the BIOS and diagnostics for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC/XT" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC/XT">IBM PC/XT</a>. In 1983 Bradley formed the Personal Systems Architecture Department.  In 1984 he helped manage development of the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_System/2" title="IBM Personal System/2">Personal System/2</a> Model 30.
</p><p>In November 1987 Bradley became manager of advanced processor design. His group developed the 486/25 Power Platform and the PS/2 Models 90 and 95.  In 1991 he became manager of systems architecture for the Entry Systems Technology group.  In 1992 he became the architecture manager for the group that developed a personal computer using the <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> <a href="/wiki/RISC" class="mw-redirect" title="RISC">RISC</a> <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moyars-Johnson_1997_Bradley_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moyars-Johnson_1997_Bradley-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993 he returned to be the manager of architecture in the PC group.
</p><p>On January 30, 2004, Bradley retired from IBM.
</p><p>Bradley wrote about the development of the IBM PC, including Control-Alt-Delete, in the August 2011 issue of the IEEE's <i>Computer</i> magazine.
</p>
