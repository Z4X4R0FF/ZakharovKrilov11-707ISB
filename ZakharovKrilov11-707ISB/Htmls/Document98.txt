<p><b>Richard D. Greenblatt</b> (born December 25, 1944) is an American <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmer</a>.  Along with <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gosper" title="Bill Gosper">Bill Gosper</a>, he may be considered to have founded the <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(programmer_subculture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (programmer subculture)">hacker</a> community,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and holds a place of distinction in the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_practice" title="Community of practice">communities</a> of the programming language <a href="/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language)" title="Lisp (programming language)">Lisp</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) <a href="/wiki/MIT_Computer_Science_and_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" title="MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">Artificial Intelligence Laboratory</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Greenblatt_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greenblatt was born in Portland, Oregon on December 25, 1944. His family moved to Philadelphia, Pennsylvania when he was a child. He later moved to Columbia, Missouri with his mother and sister when his parents divorced.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Greenblatt_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_a_hacker">Becoming a hacker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Greenblatt_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Becoming a hacker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenblatt enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> in the fall of 1962, and around his second term as an undergraduate student, he found his way to MIT's famous <a href="/wiki/Tech_Model_Railroad_Club" title="Tech Model Railroad Club">Tech Model Railroad Club</a>.  At that time, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Samson" title="Peter Samson">Peter Samson</a> had written a program in <a href="/wiki/Fortran" title="Fortran">Fortran</a> for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_709" title="IBM 709">IBM 709</a> series machines, to automate the tedious business of writing the intricate timetables for the Railroad Club's vast model train layout. Greenblatt felt compelled to implement a Fortran <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a> for the <a href="/wiki/PDP-1" title="PDP-1">PDP-1</a>, which then lacked one.  There was no computer time available to <a href="/wiki/Debugging" title="Debugging">debug</a> the compiler, or even to type it into the computer.  Years later, elements of this compiler (combined with some ideas from fellow TMRC member Steven Piner, the author of a very early <a href="/wiki/PDP-4" title="PDP-4">PDP-4</a> Fortran compiler while working for <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a>) were typed in and "showed signs of life".  However, the perceived need for a Fortran compiler had evaporated by then, so the compiler was not pursued further.  This and other experiences at TMRC, especially the influence of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Kotok" title="Alan Kotok">Alan Kotok</a>, who worked at DEC and was the junior partner of the design team for the <a href="/wiki/PDP-6" title="PDP-6">PDP-6</a> computer, led Greenblatt to  the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">AI Lab</a>, where he proceeded to become a "hacker's hacker" noted for his programming acumen as described in <a href="/wiki/Steven_Levy" title="Steven Levy">Steven Levy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hackers:_Heroes_of_the_Computer_Revolution" title="Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution">Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution</a></i>, and as acknowledged by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Jay_Sussman" title="Gerald Jay Sussman">Gerald Jay Sussman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Abelson" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold Abelson">Harold Abelson</a> when they said they were fortunate to have been apprentice programmers at the feet of <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gosper" title="Bill Gosper">Bill Gosper</a> and Richard Greenblatt.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Indeed, he spent so much time programming the <a href="/wiki/Programmed_Data_Processor" title="Programmed Data Processor">Programmed Data Processor</a> (PDP) machines there that he failed out of MIT as a first-term junior and had to take a job at a firm, Charles Adams Associates, until the AI Lab hired him about 6 months later.
</p>
<h3><span id="Lisp_Machines.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lisp_Machines,_Inc.">Lisp Machines, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Greenblatt_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lisp Machines, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1979, he and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Knight_(scientist)" title="Tom Knight (scientist)">Tom Knight</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> were the main designers of the MIT <a href="/wiki/Lisp_machine" title="Lisp machine">Lisp machine</a>. He founded <a href="/wiki/Lisp_Machines" title="Lisp Machines">Lisp Machines</a>, Inc. (later renamed Gigamos Systems), according to his vision of an ideal hacker-friendly computer company, as opposed to the more commercial ideals of <a href="/wiki/Symbolics" title="Symbolics">Symbolics</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significant_software_developed">Significant software developed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Greenblatt_(programmer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Significant software developed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was the main implementor of <a href="/wiki/Maclisp" title="Maclisp">Maclisp</a> on the <a href="/wiki/PDP-6" title="PDP-6">PDP-6</a>. He wrote <a href="/wiki/Mac_Hack" title="Mac Hack">Mac Hack</a>, the first computer program to play tournament-level chess and the first to compete in a human chess tournament.  AI skeptic <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Dreyfus" title="Hubert Dreyfus">Hubert Dreyfus</a>, who famously made the claim that computers would not be able to play high-quality chess, was beaten by the program, marking the start of "respectable" <a href="/wiki/Computer_chess" title="Computer chess">computer chess</a> performances. In 1977, unbeaten chess champion <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Fischer" title="Bobby Fischer">Bobby Fischer</a> played three games in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a> against Greenblatt's computer program, and Fischer won all of them.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Greenblatt, along with <a href="/wiki/Tom_Knight_(scientist)" title="Tom Knight (scientist)">Tom Knight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Nelson_(hacker)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stewart Nelson (hacker)">Stewart Nelson</a>, co-wrote the <a href="/wiki/Incompatible_Timesharing_System" title="Incompatible Timesharing System">Incompatible Timesharing System</a> (ITS), a highly influential <a href="/wiki/Time-sharing" title="Time-sharing">time-sharing</a> operating system for the <a href="/wiki/PDP-6" title="PDP-6">PDP-6</a> and <a href="/wiki/PDP-10" title="PDP-10">PDP-10</a> used at <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
