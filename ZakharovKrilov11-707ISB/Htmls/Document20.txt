<p><b>Fabrice Bellard</b> (<small>French pronunciation:&#160;&#8203;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[fa.bʁis bɛ.laʁ]</a></span>) is a French <a href="/wiki/Computer_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer programmer">computer programmer</a> known for writing <a href="/wiki/FFmpeg" title="FFmpeg">FFmpeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/QEMU" title="QEMU">QEMU</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Tiny_C_Compiler" title="Tiny C Compiler">Tiny C Compiler</a>. He developed <a href="/wiki/Bellard%27s_formula" title="Bellard&#39;s formula">Bellard's formula</a> for calculating single digits of <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">pi</a>. In 2012, Bellard co-founded Amarisoft, a <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> company, with Franck Spinelli.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fabrice_Bellard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bellard was born in 1972 in <a href="/wiki/Grenoble" title="Grenoble">Grenoble</a>, France and went to school in Lycée Joffre (Montpellier), where, at age 17, he created the <a href="/wiki/Executable_compression" title="Executable compression">executable compressor</a> <a href="/wiki/LZEXE" class="mw-redirect" title="LZEXE">LZEXE</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After studying at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique" title="École Polytechnique">École Polytechnique</a>, he went on to specialize at <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_nationale_sup%C3%A9rieure_des_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9communications" class="mw-redirect" title="École nationale supérieure des télécommunications">Télécom Paris</a> in 1996.
</p><p>In 1997, he discovered a new, faster formula to calculate single digits of <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">pi</a> in <a href="/wiki/Binary_numeral_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Binary numeral system">binary</a> representation, known as <a href="/wiki/Bellard%27s_formula" title="Bellard&#39;s formula">Bellard's formula</a>. It is a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Bailey%E2%80%93Borwein%E2%80%93Plouffe_formula" title="Bailey–Borwein–Plouffe formula">Bailey–Borwein–Plouffe formula</a>.
</p><p>Bellard's entries won the <a href="/wiki/IOCCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IOCCC">International Obfuscated C Code Contest</a> three times.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, he won in the category "Most Specific Output"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> for a program that implemented the modular <a href="/wiki/Fast_Fourier_Transform" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast Fourier Transform">Fast Fourier Transform</a> and used it to compute the then biggest known <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">prime number</a>, 2<sup>6972593</sup>−1.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, he won in the category "Best Abuse of the Rules" for a tiny <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compiler</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> being only 3&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kilobyte" title="Kilobyte">kB</a> in size) of a strict subset of the <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C language</a> for i386 <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>. The program itself is written in this language subset, i.e. it is <a href="/wiki/Self-hosting_(compilers)" title="Self-hosting (compilers)">self-hosting</a>.  In 2018, he won in the category "Most inflationary"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> for an image decompression program.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, he wrote the TinyCC Boot Loader, which can compile and boot a Linux kernel from source in less than 15 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, he designed a system that could act as an Analog or <a href="/wiki/DVB-T" title="DVB-T">DVB-T</a> <a href="/wiki/Digital_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital TV">Digital TV</a> transmitter by directly generating a VHF signal from a standard PC and VGA card.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, he created a minimal PC emulator written in pure <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>. The emulated hardware consists of a <a href="/wiki/32-bit" class="mw-redirect" title="32-bit">32-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a> compatible <a href="/wiki/CPU" class="mw-redirect" title="CPU">CPU</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Intel_8259" title="Intel 8259">8259 Programmable Interrupt Controller</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Intel_8253" title="Intel 8253">8254 Programmable Interrupt Timer</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Universal_asynchronous_receiver/transmitter#UART_models" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal asynchronous receiver/transmitter">16450 UART</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 31 December 2009 he claimed the world record for calculations of pi, having calculated it to nearly 2.7 trillion places in 90 days. <a href="/wiki/Slashdot" title="Slashdot">Slashdot</a> wrote: "While the improvement may seem small, it is an outstanding achievement because only a single desktop PC, costing less than US$3,000, was used—instead of a multi-million dollar supercomputer as in the previous records."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  On 2 August 2010 this record was <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_computation_of_%CF%80" title="Chronology of computation of π">eclipsed by Shigeru Kondo</a> who computed 5 trillion digits, although this was done using a server-class machine running dual Intel Xeon processors, equipped with 96&#160;GB of RAM.
</p><p>In 2011 he won an <a href="/wiki/O%27Reilly_Open_Source_Award" title="O&#39;Reilly Open Source Award">O'Reilly Open Source Award</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014 he proposed the <a href="/wiki/Better_Portable_Graphics" title="Better Portable Graphics">Better Portable Graphics</a> (BPG) image format as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/JPEG" title="JPEG">JPEG</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bpg_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bpg-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2019 he released QuickJS, a small and embeddable Javascript engine.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
